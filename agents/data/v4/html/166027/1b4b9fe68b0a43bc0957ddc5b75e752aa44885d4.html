Best Practices: Generative AI (Community Members)

What is Generative AI?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to the capability of a computer or machine to mimic human-like cognitive functions such as learning, problem solving, creativity, and decision-making.

Generative AI tools, such as chatbots, writing assistants, image generators, and embedded AI features in everyday software, can help with learning, research, and administrative tasks. However, these tools can also introduce privacy, security, and academic integrity risks if used carelessly.

These best practices are intended for students, faculty, staff, researchers, and affiliates who use generative AI tools as part of their Harvard work or studies.
 

What to Know Before Using Generative AI

When you use generative AI tools, it’s important to remember:

  • Anything you enter into an AI tool may be stored, reviewed, or reused by the service provider.
  • AI tools may be inaccurate or misleading, even when responses sound confident.
  • Some AI features are built into tools you already use (browsers, email, document editors) and may operate in the background.
  • Not all AI tools are approved for use with University data.

Common Risks

Sort
CategoryWhy It Matters
Sensitive Information Exposure
Information you enter into AI tools, such as personal details, student information, research data, or internal documents, may be stored or shared outside Harvard’s control.
Inaccurate or “Hallucinated” Outputs
AI-generated content may contain errors, outdated information, or fabricated details that can affect learning, research, or decision-making.
Bias and Fairness Concerns
AI systems may unintentionally reinforce stereotypes or unfair patterns.
Over-Reliance on AI
Depending solely on AI without human judgment can undermine learning, research quality, or fairness.
Academic or Legal Violations
Misuse of AI tools may conflict with academic integrity rules, copyright law, or Harvard policies.
Phishing and Social Engineering
Attackers leverage AI generated deceptive messages to trick users into revealing personal information.
Malicious or Unsafe AI Tools
Unsafe AI tools may collect data insecurely or introduce malware risks to your device.
Meeting Transcripts and Recordings
AI meeting tools may record or store private information without consent or adequate security.
AI Browser Data Exposure
AI-enabled browsers may retain web content, prompts, or activity for processing, creating risks if confidential or personal information is displayed or entered.
Unintended Automation
"Agent" or automation modes can act on your behalf, submitting forms or sharing data, without full review.

Best Practices

Be Careful What You Share

  • Do not enter sensitive personal information, student records, HR data, health information, or confidential University materials into public AI tools.
  • Avoid pasting unpublished research, internal communications, or proprietary documents into AI prompts.
  • When possible, use de-identified or hypothetical examples instead of real data.
  • Turn off “memory” or “agent mode” features if available, and clear chat or browsing history regularly.
     

Use Approved or Recommended Tools

  • Use AI tools that your School, program, or central IT has reviewed or approved.
  • Be cautious with browser extensions, plugins, or “free” AI tools that request broad access to your accounts or files.
  • If you’re unsure whether a tool is appropriate, ask your instructor, supervisor, or local IT support.

Check Accuracy and Bias

  • Treat AI-generated content as a starting point, not a final answer.
  • Verify important information using trusted, authoritative sources.
  • Watch for biased, stereotypical, or incomplete responses, especially in sensitive topics.

Follow Academic Integrity and Copyright Rules

  • Follow your course, program, or School’s guidance on acceptable AI use.
  • Do not submit AI-generated work as your own unless explicitly permitted.
  • Cite or acknowledge AI use when required by instructors or policies.
  • Respect copyright and licensing rules when generating or reusing content.
  • Verify security and regulatory compliance (e.g. National Defense Authorization Act)

Understand Embedded AI Features

  • Be aware that AI may be built into tools like document editors, email, search, or browsers.
  • Review settings to understand what features are enabled and what data they may use.
  • Disable AI features if you are uncomfortable with how data is handled or if they are not appropriate for your work.

Protect Your Accounts and Devices

  • Use strong, unique passwords and enable MFA for accounts used with AI tools.
  • Watch for fake or lookalike AI websites designed to steal credentials.
  • Keep your devices updated and secure, especially if using AI tools frequently.

Ask for Help When Unsure

  • If you’re unsure whether an AI tool is appropriate for a task, ask before using it.
  • Contact your instructor, supervisor, School Security & Privacy Officer (SPSO), or IT support for guidance.
  • For sensitive data or unusual use cases, consult your SPSO or PrivSec.
