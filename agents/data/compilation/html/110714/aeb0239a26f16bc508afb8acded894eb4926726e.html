FAQ

If your question is not answered below, you can contact the AI Council using this form.

General

What is AI?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a branch of computer science focused on creating machines and software that can perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence, such as understanding language, recognizing patterns, solving problems, and making decisions. Many AI systems learn from data, adapt to new inputs, and can improve their performance over time.

AI is also a trope in science fiction (like aliens and zombies), and it is problematically easy to apply expectations from this fictional sense of AI when discussing real-world technical systems. The AI Council is focused on transforming the results from AI research into normal information technologies (comparable to email and wifi).

To learn more, the UC AI Council has developed a glossary of AI and AI-adjacent terms.  

What is Generative AI?

Generative AI (GenAI) refers to a subset of AI systems designed to create new, original content—such as text, images, audio, or other media—based on patterns learned from large datasets. These systems generate novel outputs in response to user prompts, distinguishing them from other AI that may only analyze or classify data.

What forms of AI am I likely to experience? 
  • A chatbot is a type of conversational AI designed to simulate human-like conversations, often used to automate customer support or answer questions. Chatbots interact with users via text or voice, providing responses based on programmed rules or AI models, and can operate around the clock to assist users.
  • An (AI) agent is an autonomous software program or system that interacts with its environment, collects data, and makes decisions to achieve specific goals set by humans. AI agents can sense their surroundings, analyze information, and independently choose actions to optimize outcomes, such as answering customer queries or navigating a self-driving car.
  • A robot is an autonomous machine capable of sensing its environment, processing information to make decisions, and performing physical actions in the real world. Robots combine sensors, computation, and actuators to carry out tasks ranging from cleaning floors to assembling products in factories.
What tools are currently available for use at UC Santa Cruz? 

While AI is embedded in several tools licensed on campus (such as predictive text and autocorrect functions Google Docs, spam filtering in Gmail, and grouping answers in Gradescope), the campus does not currently have a dedicated generative AI platform. The AI Council is exploring options for procuring a platform.

Training is available for instructors, students, and staff. To learn more about options, see the Training and Guides page.

How does UCSC ensure inclusive and ethical AI development?

Through application of the UC AI principles and the meticulous procurement process, UCSC emphasizes diversity, equity, and inclusion in AI research and education and promotes responsible AI practices.

How does UCSC balance the benefits and risks of AI?

Through the procurement process (purchasing, security testing, accessibility testing), training (guides and workshops), and use, UCSC aims to harness AI’s potential for learning, research, and administration while actively addressing risks like misuse, bias, and privacy concerns.

Learn about laws and UC policies on the UC Office of the President website that govern use and deployment of AI on UC campuses.

Privacy and Security

How does the UC classify data? 

The UC classifies data through four levels of protection with “P4” being the highest level of protection required, and “P1” being the lowest. Understanding these levels of protection is critical for safely and securely using artificial intelligence. 

How do I keep UCSC data safe when using AI? 

Unless approved by IT Security, sensitive or confidential data, such as personal student information, should not be included in AI prompts (or any data uploaded in a prompt, such as documents, spreadsheets, images, etc.) to protect privacy and comply with campus and legal policies. For use in AI prompts, to protect the privacy of individuals and institutional data, only include information that is within the protection levels of P1 or P2. 

What are examples of text or data that I CAN put in a prompt? 

Any text or data that contains information that is at protection level P1 or P2 can be placed in a prompt. These include: 

  • Your emails, calendar information, meeting notes or other business records and documentation (not containing P3 or P4 data); 
  • Research data using publicly available data, or research data that has been de-identified; or, 
  • Publicly available institutional information such as websites, the course catalog, policies, guidance, or regulations, curricular pathways, position descriptions, etc. 
What are examples of text or data that I SHOULD NOT put in a prompt? 

Any text or data that contains information that is at protection level P3 or P4. These include:

  • Student education records (these are protected by FERPA); 
  • Intellectual property that is not owned by the person writing the prompt; 
  • Personnel records such as performance evaluations or faculty merit and promotion cases; 
  • Medical records; 
  • Student disability or academic accommodation information; 
  • Student financial aid information;
  • Any information labelled as Attorney/client privileged information; or,
  • Federally or state regulated data that may cause harm to the university should unauthorized access take place.
How secure is the data used and processed by AI? 

Data used and processed by AI using publicly available tools like ChatGPT, Gemini and CoPilot should not be considered secure. The default setting on most free tools is for data to be used for training purposes, which can compromise person or institutional data. 

Some of these tools include functionality to turn off training (e.g., with ChatGPT users can turn off “Improve the model for everyone”). When this functionality is available, we encourage you to use it.

Teaching and Learning

As a student, can I use AI in my coursework? 

At UC Santa Cruz, instructors set policy on use of AI at the individual course level. In some cases, a policy might be set at the level of an assignment. If you are unsure what the policy is, before using AI, you should always reread the course syllabus. If the syllabus does not include an explicit policy, you should contact the instructor. 

Is student use of AI considered plagiarism? 

It depends on the instructor’s policy in the course. If you are unsure of the policy, reread the syllabus and then (if you are still unsure) contact the instructor. 

As a student, is it appropriate to use materials from a course I’m in (such as assignments, syllabi, readings) in an AI prompt?

Unless you have permission from the instructor, it is not appropriate to prompt AI using any intellectual property (such as course materials) that does not belong to you.

As an instructor, how will I know if it is appropriate for me to use AI in my teaching? 

The AI Council encourages academic divisions, departments, and programs to discuss the appropriateness of using generative AI in teaching and learning. In some fields the use of AI in teaching will be highly likely due to changing expectations for faculty and students, and in other fields the use of AI in teaching may not be appropriate, while still in others use may be appropriate in some ways but not in others. 

As an instructor, am I permitted to use AI tools to detect suspected student use of AI? 

AI detection tools may only be used at UC Santa Cruz if the tool stores data only within systems managed by UC Santa Cruz and prevents outside access, the tool is contracted through campus Purchasing, or if you obtain prior approval from students. Without meeting one of these criteria, the use of AI detectors may violate the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), which prevents the unauthorized sharing of student work. This guidance is explained in detail in a March 20, 2023 communication from Vice Provosts Richard Hughey (Undergraduate Education) and Peter Biehl (Graduate Studies) to instructors.

“Student work should never be submitted to web-based detection tools, such as GPTZero and other tools, unless one of the following three criteria is satisfied:

  • The tool is hosted by UC Santa Cruz (the campus or an individual unit) and data is protected from external access.
  • The tool is contracted with UC Santa Cruz (the campus or an individual unit) through Purchasing in a way that protects student information.
  • Students have provided affirmative approval for their work to be submitted to the tool.”

If you suspect student misuse of AI, we encourage you to consult with the Teaching and Learning Center for guidance or with the Academic Integrity Office for reporting. 

As an instructor, are there specific resources to support teaching in the age of generative AI? 

Yes, the Teaching and Learning Center (TLC) offers workshops and guidance on approaching the use of AI in teaching, writing course-level AI policies, and minimizing unauthorized student use of AI. 

What should I do if I have concerns about AI use in my class or department?

Contact your instructor, department chair, divisional leadership, the AI Council, or relevant administrative office to discuss concerns or seek guidance.

Research and Mentoring

Can AI be used to write grant applications? 

It depends. Many funding agencies have added restrictions on how AI can be used in proposals. You should review the instructions provided by the funding agency and ask for guidance if it is not explicitly addressed.

When use of AI is allowed, there are several helpful ways to use it, including the following: 

  • Spotting disfluencies and other inconsistencies in the proposal text;
  • Identifying similar concepts or approaches in adjacent disciplines outside of the PIs background;
  • Coming up with low-jargon explanations for otherwise technical plans;
  • Coming up with alternate motivating stories for projects;
  • Breaking down large projects into smaller ones that can be organized into timelines;
  • Anticipating reviewer critiques sketching responses to them;
  • Aligning the proposal narrative to specific details mentioned in a solicitation document;
  • Identifying extramural stakeholder groups;
  • Using it as a moderator among different collaborators/groups;
  • Identifying relevant aspects of grant proposals;
  • Organizing narratives in clear and simple ways;
  • Searching citations and references in the literature; or 
  • Creating draft summaries of similar work. 

A note of caution: it is possible that AI-generated responses may include passages verbatim from other sources, so quoting an AI-generated response may crossover to traditional plagiarism.

Can AI be used in the research process?

Yes, certain tools can be used as AI agents that would either support data exploration or help create tools for the research process. Some possibilities include:

  • Exploring data with tools such Vibe coding; 
  • Creating synthetic data that can be used to test processes; 
  • Identifying weak points in research pipelines; 
  • Searching for existing similar tools, policies, or considerations regarding the research matter; or 
  • Simulating and visualizing relevant data.
Can AI be used for human subjects research?

Researchers who use AI in their work with human subjects should be aware of the potential risks and limitations, and should ensure that research participants are fully informed. If you are considering using AI in human subjects research, contact UCSC’s IRB office at orca@ucsc.edu.

How do I know if the research results provided by AI are accurate?

They may not be, and even small wording changes in the prompts may alter your results. Be skeptical of the results, and do your due diligence in fact-checking information. Review outputs to validate that the data is accurate, and that you can trace them back to original credible sources.

Can AI be used in student and postdoc mentoring?

Some faculty have reported on ways that using AI for mentoring has been helpful to them. These include the following: 

Communication

  • Rephrase emails and check for clarity and cordiality; 
  • Reverse outlining existing writing to reveal what it seems to want to communicate; 
  • Check writing against an outline, or help enact feedback given on an outline to the expanded version of the document; 
  • Help your mentees decode your perhaps too-dense and discipline-specific feedback on their own schedule; 
  • Design key figures and write the automation scripts to generate them from experimental data; or 
  • Obtaining suggestions on how to address an issue or request.

Project Design

  • Decompose large tasks into actionable steps; 
  • Generate code to implement toy versions (conceptual simplifications) of your research systems; or 
  • Generate code that can be run to generate sample data in the correct format to aid in developing other project-specific research infrastructure.

In the Workplace

What benefits are there for using AI in the workplace? 

Staff have shared a variety of ways that using AI in the workplace has helped them, including through the tasks listed below. Note, you must always review the outputs of AI for accuracy. 

  • Communications:
    • Adjusting tone
    • Modifying structure
    • Improving clarity
    • Identifying redundancies
    • Checking for consistency, etc. 
  • Data analysis:
    • Analyzing
    • Interpreting
    • Visualizing
    • Identifying gaps in data, etc.
  • Accessibility:
    • Generating alt text for images.
  • Automation:
    • Automating some routine tasks through use of tools like chatbots. 
What data can I put into a GPT?

Data privacy is critically important. If you aren’t already familiar with data protection levels, see this guide. In general, unless you are using a university-approved tool that has been sanctioned for use with P3 or P4 data, you should never put this level of data into an AI prompt. This includes material from faculty merit and promotion cases, and recruitment applications. 

It is okay to enter text or data that contains information that is at protection level P1 or P2. These include:

  • Emails, calendar information, meeting notes or other business records and documentation (not containing P3 or P4 data).
  • Research data using publicly available data, or research data that has been de-identified.
  • Publicly available institutional information such as websites, the course catalog, policies, guidance, or regulations, curricular pathways, position descriptions, etc.

UC Santa Cruz does not currently have licensed AI tools, and individuals and units are experimenting with using GPT tools like Claude or ChatGPT that are readily available and can be purchased using a procard. See the question below about purchasing an AI tool at your department or division level.

How should I indicate that generative AI was used in my work? 

If you are using generative AI, the AI Council encourages the use to be acknowledged. Acknowledgement is often done through transparently stating whether the use was an AI-enhancement, or fully AI-generated, and includes the prompt(s). How can I learn more about AI?

There are many ways to learn more about AI, and we encourage you to take advantage of being on a university campus by engaging with centers, clusters, or clubs on artificial intelligence. In addition to these intellectual engagements, there are several options available for training.

I have an idea for using AI. Where should I go next?

We encourage you to experiment, build, and play with AI within the UC framework for data protection and security. If you have an idea, talk with your supervisor or department chair or manager about your idea and a plan for experimenting and testing it. In those conversations, explore the type of data that you would like to use with AI and check it against the data protection used by the campus. As a reminder, you should only use P1 or P2 level data with AI tools.
