The recent growth in generative artificial intelligence (AI) tools has quickly changed the education landscape. Below are some frequently asked questions and suggestions from the TLHub to help you be on the forefront of these tools and their impact on your course. Please note that these are emerging tools, and we will continue to provide new and updated resources as we develop our guidance on the intersection of teaching and AI.

Generative AI Course Policies

What is the GSBs policy on student AI use for coursework?

MBA/MSx courses: Instructors may not ban student use of AI tools for take-home coursework, including assignments and exams. Instructors may choose whether to allow student use of AI tools for in-class work, including exams. Except for in-class exams, students should be allowed to use AI tools in cases where they are able to use the internet and electronic devices. In-class assignments for which electronic devices are prohibited inherently also prohibit use of the internet, AI tools, tablets, laptops, calculators, etc.

PhD/Undergraduate courses:Follow the Generative AI Policy Guidance from Stanford’s Office of Community Standards.

Note that:

  • Students are expected to cite all resources, including generative AI tools, used in the preparation of academic work.
  • It is recommended that students use secure, Stanford-approved tools such as the Stanford AI Playground or Google Gemini and NotebookLM (secure only when logged in with SUNet ID).
  • Students are expected to follow UITs guidelines on Responsible AI at Stanford, which limits the use of sensitive data with third-party AI tools, including for take-home coursework.

Wondering how to plan your assignments accordingly? See the FAQs on Coursework in the AI Era.

What should my course policy on AI use include?

In your course policy on AI use, you may want to consider the following:

  • Explain whether students may use AI tools in class, and under what circumstances, alongside a rationale for your policy.
  • Highlight any sensitive and/or proprietary data that students will handle in your course and restrictions on how these materials may be used with AI tools. This should include whether or not students may input instructor-created course materials (e.g., slide decks, assignment prompts) into commercial AI tools. [Not sure what to recommend? See “What are Stanford’s data security guidelines for AI use?” below.]
  • Describe how students should cite or report AI use in your course. [See “How can I ask students to cite or report their AI use?” below.]

For syllabus statement templates and more tips, see our resource on Course Policies on Generative AI Use.

State your policy in your course syllabus and the Course Policies and Norms form, and then follow up in class with your students.

How can I ask students to cite or report their AI use?

GSB students are expected to cite all resources, including generative AI tools, used in the preparation of academic work.

Nevertheless, many students are hesitant to report AI use. It can help to ask all students to report how they used AI (instead of whether they used AI). Asking students to regularly report their AI use can help normalize transparent AI use.

Here are some suggestions for how students can acknowledge AI use:

  • Submit chat logs associated with an assignment or exam
  • Include statements with coursework to disclose how AI was used

Sample statement: Claude was used to generate data visualizations and an initial written draft. Substantial revisions were made by the author. The ideas discussed in this report are those of the author alone.

  • Reflect on how they used AI for the assignment and what was or wasnt helpful for them
  • Cite AI use according to standards in your field or industry

Find more in our resource on Course Policies on Generative AI Use.

What are Stanford's data security guidelines for AI use?

When using any AI tool for Stanford-related work, follow UITs guidelines on Responsible AI at Stanford.

When using third-party commercial tools: If you (or your students) use third-party generative AI tools that Stanford does not support (such as ChatGPTor Claude), note that such tools should be used only for low risk data that can be made public. For example, when using these tools, Stanford users should avoid inputting materials containing students personal information and proprietary or copyrighted materials (which may include case studies, course assignments, data sets, and more).

When using Stanford-approved tools: Faculty, staff, and students may use the Stanford AI Playground, Google Gemini, and NotebookLM (when logged in with SUNet ID). These secure tools are approved for use with low and moderate risk data. This includes most Stanford course materials. Learn more about UIT’s data risk classifications and generative AI at Stanford.

See also our FAQs on Using AI Tools below.

Coursework and AI

What are some guiding principles around AI and teaching?

AI tools have been evolving so quickly that its difficult to pin down best practices for teaching and learning. Ultimately, how AI tools are best used (or not used) in your course will depend on your course goals.

In some cases students may be able to use AI tools to reach new heights, while at other times students may use AI tools to shortcut their learning. Here are two top ideas that are sometimes synergistic and other times at odds:

  • Authentic work today often includes using generative AI tools. Todays students will go on to careers in which AI technology will be commonly used, and educators want students to leave the classroom prepared for that work.
  • Leading institutions aim to educate students for the long-term, providing them with lasting principles and problem-solving capabilities. Developing this knowledge and these skills may not always rely on or require the use of AI tools.

Not sure where to start? Below are some top recommendations that are useful for any course approach:

  • Students want clear guidelines on AI use for your course, as well as the “why.”Clearly explain your course approach to AI. Then, connect your choices or recommendations to the learning goals of the course, industry standards (e.g., publication standards), or common industry cases (e.g., needing to give thoughtful/persuasive/clear input on the spot during board meetings).
  • Test your assignments with AI tools regularly. AI tools are evolving at a rapid pace, and the nature of what an AI tool can produce for your coursework may change every quarter that you teach. For current Stanford-approved AI tools, see the FAQs on Using AI Tools.
  • Learn more about how GSB instructors and students have been using AIin the classroom in the FAQs below or by viewing recent survey data (SUNet login required).

Looking for more specifics? See the FAQs below on assessing student learning and promoting deep learning.

How can I best assess student learning in the AI era?

When students use AI to produce assignments, how can you distinguish what theyve actually learned from what AI did for them? Gathering a wide range of evidence of your students learning can help.

See Assessing Student Learning for a deeper dive into this topic, including examples and assessment tips.

Step 1. Set clear learning goals: Develop clear goals for what you want your students to learn in your course and connect these to real-world tasks of experts in your field or industry. Then share and discuss these goals with your students to help increase their motivation.

Step 2. Gather a range of evidence for learning

Observe learning in action: Frequent assessments and checkpoints, especially in class, help capture student learning throughout the quarter. Examples include:

  • Midterm exams, weekly quizzes, or presentations, held in class
  • Strategic follow-up questions during discussion that push beyond pre-prepared study questions
  • Polling to collect student responses to a few questions about that day’s material
  • Peer evaluation of a role-play, presentation, or project update
  • Recordings of prep assignments (such as interviews, negotiations, or group sessions), taken with permission

Capture the learning behind the deliverable: Complement industry-standard deliverables (like memos, models, analyses, decks, and strategic recommendations) with other evidence to assess the true extent of student learning. Examples include:

  • Short in-class exercises or quizzes that test the same concepts students incorporate into their out-of-class assignments or projects
  • Collect regular status reports and change logs for large projects
  • Have students document and reflect on their AI use, including what contributed to a tangible sense of learning for them and what didn’t work well

Step 3. Align grading with evidence of learning: Your gradebook is a way to reinforce the specific behaviors, skills, and competencies you value most for your students. Consider whether the final grade accurately reflects where learning happens in your course, and how you can reward the thinking as much as the product.

How can I promote students deep learning and thinking in the AI era?
As AI tool use becomes ubiquitous, how can we help students avoid offloading their learning to AI tools? Here are some tips to promote students deep learning and thinking:
Leverage AI to extend learning. Students today can have more information, analysis, and personalizable tools available at their fingertips, and we can lean in to these AI affordances. For example:
  • Expect more (research, prototypes, examples, etc.)
  • Follow up with discussion questions that push beyond pre-prepared answers (ask for justification, counter-arguments, connections to others comments, etc.)
  • Include real-world tasks that use AI authentically, in ways that students will encounter in the workplace
  • Lead a discussion about the affordances and challenges of AI in your field
  • Have students use AI tools for simulated tasks, role-play, getting feedback and coaching, or other personalized learning of course concepts
Promote deep learning and reduce cognitive off-loading. Students today may be tempted to engage less in productive struggle needed to deeply learn new material, but we can incorporate guardrails to support deep learning. For example:
  • Explain the “why” behind your coursework and any AI guidelines, especially if AI can generate a good answer in your course
  • Hold students accountable with high standards of in-class work (e.g., in-person exams, real-time discussion, debate, or problem solving activities)
  • Encourage critique by having students push back on an AIs output or ask an AI tool for feedback on their work
  • Emphasize process over product with iterative work, such as drafts and feedback checkpoints
  • Ask students to discuss how they approached their work, not just what conclusion they reached
What are some examples of how GSB students have used AI tools in their coursework?

View survey data to learn more about how GSB instructors and students have been using AI in the classroom (SUNet login required).

Most GSB students use AI tools regularly, including with workflows that combine AI tools for different parts of a project or learning routine. GSB students have reported exploring or engaging with AI tools for tasks like:

  • Summarizing material (developing key takeaways, asking questions about a reading, compiling meeting notes, etc.)
  • Research (finding relevant news articles, digesting academic scholarship, etc.)
  • Brainstorming and ideation (developing potential talking points, brainstorming alternative approaches to a problem, etc.)
  • Writing and editing (refining writing mechanics, drafting paragraphs from an outline, etc.)
  • Studying, reviewing, and getting feedback (checking answers, getting alternative explanations, creating study materials, turning long-form articles into podcasts, etc.)
  • Performing creative tasks (generating image, video, or data visualizations; writing a childrens story, etc.)
  • Drafting and troubleshooting code (e.g., Claude, GitHub CoPilot)

Encourage students to critically evaluate all AI-generated content, no matter how they use the tools, and refer students to your AI use policy to let them know how to report their AI use.

For more, review the event highlights or watch the recording of Student Voices: Exploring AI in the GSB Classroom.

Using AI Tools

What AI tools are available for the GSB community to use?

AI Playground: Stanford users have access to the Stanford AI Playground, a platform for safely interacting with multiple advanced and widely used AI models, including ChatGPT and Claude. This is a secure tool for Stanford users and may be used with low and medium risk data.

Google Gemini and NotebookLM: Stanford users have access to Google Gemini and NotebookLM. These tools are secure for Stanford users when logged in with SUNet ID and may be used with low and medium risk data.

Nectir AI: This secure and customizable chatbot tool that integrates directly into Canvas, is now available (GSB only). Visit this Canvas site for how-to guides and to interact with sample bots, or contact us to learn more.

Commercial tools: The Stanford community may also use commercially available AI tools for university-related work, including student coursework, provided they follow the guidelines in Stanford ITs resource on Responsible AI at Stanford.

Additional resources and tips:

  • For sample syllabus statements to guide students’ use of AI tools, see our resource on Course Policies on Generative AI Use.
  • If you would like to use a tool in your course that is not currently approved, see the adoption process for new technologies for GSB courses.
What is the Stanford AI Playground?

The Stanford AI Playground is a generative AI platform for Stanford students, faculty, and staff. Access the platform using your Stanford credentials and learn more in the AI Playground Quick Start Guide.

Top features and tips for using the AI Playground:

  • Access multiple advanced and widely used AI models, including ChatGPT 5 and Claude 4 Sonnet. Switch between AI models using the dropdown menus at the top of the screen.
  • The tool is safe and secure to use. You can use the tool with low and medium risk data. Information you share with the AI Playground is not accessible by other people using the tool and is not saved by the vendor or used to train the AI models.
  • Test your assignments using the AI Playground regularly. AI tools are evolving at a rapid pace, and the quality of what an AI tool can produce for your assignments or coursework may change every quarter that you teach.
What materials can I use with or input into Stanford-approved AI tools when teaching?

The Stanford AI Playground, Google Gemini, and NotebookLM are safe to use with course and student materials containing low and moderate risk data. Note that Gemini and NotebookLM are secure only when logged in with SUNet ID.

Instructor-generated materials.Stanford-approved toolsmay be used with materials such as:

  • Exams
  • Assignments
  • Lecture notes
  • Slides

Student-generated materials, including completed assignments and other coursework or student survey and evaluation responses. These materials have restrictions on how they may be used with Stanford-approved tools:

  • Instructors may input student content for uses such as generating takeaways about student learning or highlighting gaps in understanding, but Stanford discourages using AI for grading at this time.

Published and proprietary materials, including case studies and data sets. These materials have restrictions on how they may be used with Stanford-approved tools:

  • Some published and proprietary materials, including some library materials or research data, may not be used with AI tools. Please consult with the Business Library for specific usage guidelines.
  • Any use of published or proprietary materials in the classroom, including when using AI tools, should align with fair use principles. Please consult with Stanfords Copyright and Fair Use Center for further information on specific classroom use cases.

High-risk data. The AI Playground, Google Gemini, and NotebookLM are not safe for any data classified as high risk according to Stanford UITs risk classifications.

Can I still use ChatGPT or other AI tools?

Stanford community members may use third-party AI tools that are not Stanford supported, such as ChatGPT and Claude, according to UITs guidelines on Responsible AI at Stanford, but only for low risk data that can be made public. When using these tools, faculty and staff should avoid inputting materials containing students personal information and proprietary or copyrighted materials (which may include case studies, course assignments, data sets, and more), or any information that may be classified as moderate or high risk according to Stanford UITs risk classifications.

AI Tools and Academic Honesty

Can I use AI detection software to detect student AI use?

Under the honor code, faculty may use AI detection software if they provide students with clear, advance notice, as in the Office of Community Standards Tips for Faculty & TAs.

If you are considering using AI detection software, consider the following guidelines:

  • AI detection software cannot be used as a deterrent for student AI use in take-home coursework, since GSB instructors may not ban student AI use for take-home coursework.
  • Stanford does not have approved general licenses for plagiarism monitoring tools (a common example is Turnitin). We recommend faculty use AI detector tools with a high degree of caution, if they choose to use such software, because AI detector tools can vary widely in their accuracy and can produce both false negatives and false positives. Users can also bypass detection tools by revising AI-generated text and testing their work in AI detector tools themselves to check detection likelihood.
  • Each AI detection tool differs in its approach to privacy and how inputted material may be shared with third parties. If you choose to use an AI detection tool, avoid inputting information that should not be made public, according to UITs guidelines for Responsible AI at Stanford. This includes personal, sensitive, confidential, or proprietary information that may be contained within students coursework, including student names.
How can I identify student work that has used AI-generated content without citation?

You may ask your students to cite some or all of their use of AI-generated content. (See “How can I ask students to cite or report their AI use?” above, as well asour resource on Course Policies on Generative AI Use.)

If you are concerned about student use of AI-generated content that is not properly cited, consider the following:

  • Test your course assignments using one or several AI tools. Use a secure tool such as theStanford AI Playground, Google Gemini, or NotebookLM (secure only when logged in with SUNet ID). This will provide the clearest sense of how an AI-generated output may compare to high-quality student work in your course. Note that such tools do not generally produce the same output each time a question is posed.
  • Some common strategies for detecting plagiarism may be useful for identifying improperly cited use of AI tools. These include looking for generic or repetitive language, large shifts in writing voice or style, improper and missing citations or facts, or if a student cant explain how they arrived at an answer or made choices in producing an assignment.
  • AI detection software is available, but has significant limitations. See above for more.
What else can I do to deter students from using AI tools in ways that might undermine their learning?

There are some methods you can use to minimize students using learning shortcuts when completing assignments. For design practices that help improve learning and encourage deep student engagement, see the FAQs on Coursework in the AI Era.

Getting Started with AI

What are generative AI tools?

Generative AI is a type of artificial intelligence technology that can generate new content based on patterns and information gathered from sets of sample materials. Heres what else to know about this technology:

  • The most well-known generative AI tools are chatbots (such as the Stanford AI Playground; and commercial tools, e.g., ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Google Gemini, or Claude). These are AI-based text generation tools that users interact with using a chat interface. Users type a question or prompt, and the chatbot responds with a cohesive and creative written answer. The chatbot can then refine or adjust its responses based on how the user continues to interact with the tool, such as by continuing to pose questions or asking for changes to the chatbots output.
  • Other generative AI tools produce many different outputs such as images (e.g., DALL-E or Midjourney), code (e.g., GitHub CoPilot, Cursor), slides (e.g., Gamma, Canva), or internet search results (e.g., Perplexity).
  • AI tools use training data (sample materials or data) to inform the content they generate. The quality of AI-generated content can vary widely, in part based on the quality or focus of the training data. For example, a tool trained on large amounts of internet content may be able to respond to a prompt in many different and creative ways, but the tools output may also reflect the varying accuracy or biases embedded within the sample internet content. In addition, AI tools sometimes prioritize completing or responding to a users prompt over accuracy of information, and can sometimes make up information entirely (known as hallucinating).
  • For definitions of common AI terms and additional explanations of how AI chatbots work, see Stanford Teaching Commons resource on defining AI and chatbots.
How can I learn more about AI tools?
  • Stanford UITs AI Playground and GenAI Overview provides a safe, secure, and Stanford-approved tool to test various AI models with tips and resources available.
  • Stanford Teaching Commons Artificial Intelligence Teaching Guide provides a step by step guide to generative AI tools in the higher education context, including exercises to experiment with AI tools.
