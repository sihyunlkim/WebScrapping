Welcome to

The Wildcat Connection

Celebrating teaching, learning, and the people who make it real.

 

At Western Nevada College, the Center for Teaching Excellence utilizes the ASWN Wildcat Studio, located in the Joe Dini Building in the ASWN offices (pictured above), to interview and conduct a podcast with instructors and community members on the appreciation that can be shown for their hard work and teaching strategies.

 

In this episode of the Wildcat Connection, produced by Western Nevada College’s Center for Teaching Excellence, we sit down with Geology professor Winnie Kortemeier to explore her journey as an educator. In the episode, she shares her love for rocks, how impactful teaching is at WNC for the students, and what that means for teaching faculty on a personal level.
 
"When I talk to students, they don't care that we have the fanciest bathrooms, they care about the individual learning experience they recieve here. And I get to give students the same education that I got at a fancy liberal arts college for a whole lot cheaper!" - Winnie Kortemeier

Dr. Patrick Bell is a native Nevadan who grew up in the Carson Valley and is passionate about serving the needs of Nevada’s rural communities. He has over 25 years of teaching experience across elementary, middle, and high school math and science classrooms. At the college level, Dr. Bell has taught education courses as an adjunct across NSHE institutions, including UNR, TMCC, and WNC. Most recently, he has served as a full-time education instructor at Western Nevada College, preparing pre-education students to transfer into university teacher preparation programs. Dr. Bell has also held leadership roles at the school, district, and state levels, including work with the Nevada Department of Education in career and technical education and the state’s school accountability system. In addition, he serves as a student teaching supervisor for the University of Nevada, Reno, evaluating teacher candidates in the final year of their preparation program, and teaches graduate-level education courses at the university. Dr. Bell is deeply committed to education and instills in his students the belief that teaching is fundamentally about making a meaningful difference in the lives of the students you serve.

In this episode of the Wildcat Connection, we sit down with Criminal Justice Professor Gregory Sly to discuss his extensive experience working in the criminal justice system and how this led him to his current role teaching WNC's students in the Criminal Justice Studies Program.  Following a rewarding career of over thirty years in law enforcement and probation services, Greg retired in 2013 and thereafter, sought work at WNC to continue his service to the community.  Initially appointed by WNC as a Grant Project Manager in 2014, Greg managed U.S. Department of Labor grant awards for four years, assisting the Workforce, Career and Technical Education Division in developing career preparation programs in many of WNC's educational areas of study.  In 2018, Greg was provided with the opportunity to transition from grants administration to teaching in WNC's Criminal Justice Studies Program, a position that Greg describes as the best job he has ever had.  Now a tenured Community College Professor and Program Coordinator, Greg works alongside six other WNC Adjunct Faculty Instructors, teaching criminal justice coursework for students interested in the criminal justice system and the many careers available in law enforcement, corrections, community corrections, and the courts.  

"Assisting the WNC students in their discovery, preparation, and career placement in the criminal justice system is extremely rewarding.  I continuously look forward to seeing how our talented students will lead the CRJ system into the future." - Gregory S. Sly

Rachel Stiff is a mixed media artist and tenured faculty member at Western Nevada College in Carson City. Originally from rural Montana, her early experiences in vast, open landscapes continue to shape her abstract interpretations of the modern West. She holds an MFA from the University of Arizona and a BFA from the University of Montana.

Working across drawing, painting, and printmaking, Stiff investigates the evolving intersections of the natural and built environments, particularly in high desert terrain. Her work captures nuanced shifts in light, color, and geological time, offering a contemplative lens on place and perception. Her work has been exhibited widely throughout the American West and is held in the collections of Traktor Films and the Marjorie Barrick Museum of Art.

JoAnne Northrup, Executive Director of the Nerman Museum, describes her work as “powerful… evocative of the natural world yet stopping short of depiction,” placing her within the lineage of Mitchell and Frankenthaler, yet grounded in a distinctly contemporary perspective.

John A. Duerk is a tenured professor of political science and history who believes that intellectual curiosity and constructive disagreement are central to a meaningful education at the collegiate level.  In courses such as PSC 101: Introduction to American Politics, his students examine a variety of issues — from immigration and 'the right to be forgotten' to political tribalism and affirmative action — to better understand their complexity.  Moreover, he supports forms of civic engagement that have the capacity to improve people's quality of life.  Currently, he and his colleagues are working on an initiative to teach students the importance of agency within a political system composed of institutions that exist, in part, to meet our needs. 
"Nobody has time for every issue, but everybody can make time for one issue.  So, choose an issue that you find important and try to make your community better reflect your values." - Dr. John Duerk

Dr. J. Kyle Dalpe has spent more than 28 years in higher education helping students find opportunity, purpose, and success. As President of Western Nevada College, he leads a vibrant community college with campuses in Carson City, Fallon, and Minden, where students truly “start here and go anywhere.”
Under his leadership, WNC has launched new programs, expanded access, boosted inclusion, and seen enrollment climb to its highest level in more than a decade. The college has also reached new milestones in fundraising to support students and campus development — thanks to the combined efforts of its dedicated team and community partners.
President Dalpe believes community colleges are life-changers. They are where dreams take root, careers begin, and second chances flourish. As he often says: “Community colleges are the door to opportunity - people walk through our doors to change their lives.”

In 2018 Margaret received her Masters degree in Instructional Design. It was the first time she had done course work towards a degree that was not in ASL. It was challenging, but she was so glad to have switched gears. At that same time, she was teaching in person classes for Western Nevada College part-time, and many of the students asked if she ever thought about teaching ASL online. Considering both her BS and MA were online degrees, she decided to design her first online ASL course.

She worked on the course for over a period of a year, spending hours developing teaching videos, additional resources, and an outline on how she would teach each course. She started with ASL 1 and 2, then later developed 3 and 4. One of her goals was to be able to reach students everywhere, especially rural areas, homebound students, students with various disabilities and more. So far the program has reached students in 28 different states, with more being added every semester. The online courses offer opportunities for so many different kinds of students, High School students, single Moms and Dads, Full-time workers, military, teachers needing CEU’s, speech pathologists, and people who are simply interested in learning the language. She found teaching ASL online to be incredibly rewarding, and that having this online forum creates a pathway for so many students, who otherwise would not have access, to to learn this beautiful language.
