AI Ethics: Principles, Issues, and Future Impacts

By University of North Dakota/November 10, 2025

Would you trust a machine to decide who gets a loan or which resume deserves a second look? Artificial intelligence is already helping make those decisions.

Request Information

From diagnosing diseases to curating what you see on social media, AI is part of everyday life.
Its influence is growing quickly, along with concerns about bias, accountability, and transparency.

This is where AI ethics comes in. It focuses on creating and using intelligent systems in ways that are fair, safe, and aligned with human values.

As AI becomes more powerful, the need for clear ethical standards is more urgent than ever.
The choices made today will shape both technology and society in the years ahead.

Key Takeaways

  • AI ethics helps prevent real-world harm, such as biased hiring tools and over-policing, which have already impacted lives and trust in AI systems.
  • Building ethical AI requires action at every stage, including diverse design teams, regular audits, and clear accountability structures.
  • Global regulation is gaining momentum, with the EU AI Act and UNESCO guidelines shaping how AI should be governed worldwide.
  • UND equips students with the tools to lead in ethical AI, offering courses that explore the social, legal, and moral questions surrounding emerging technologies.

What Is AI Ethics?

AI ethics is the study of how artificial intelligence should be designed, used, and managed to benefit people and society, as opposed to damaging them. It involves applying moral principles to guide decisions made by individuals and organizations that build or use AI systems. These principles help ensure that AI respects human rights, avoids harm, and promotes fairness.

AI ethics focuses on making sure these technologies are used in ways that support transparency, accountability, and trust. For example, an AI system used in hiring should not favor certain groups or filter out qualified candidates based on biased data. Without ethical oversight, AI can reinforce inequality, make harmful decisions, or operate in ways that are difficult to question or correct.

AI ethics is not handled by one group alone. It brings together computer scientists, policymakers, business leaders, lawyers, and members of the public. Each group plays a role in shaping how AI is built and used.

A recent global study found that 73 %  of consumers trust content produced by generative AI, though awareness of risks like misuse is notably low. This highlights the importance of ensuring ethical foundations keep pace with AI’s rapid adoption.

Core Principles of Ethical AI

To guide AI responsibly, we must anchor its development in clear ethical principles. These core ideas act as a moral framework that helps ensure AI supports human well-being, respects rights, and works transparently. They include fairness, accountability, transparency, privacy, and safety. These are not just theoretical ideals. They shape how AI is trained, tested, and used in real-world settings. 

Fairness helps prevent discrimination, while accountability ensures someone is answerable for outcomes. Each principle helps build public trust and keeps AI aligned with values that matter to individuals and communities. Without them, AI risks causing harm or reinforcing injustice.

Fairness and Bias Prevention

AI systems must treat all individuals equally, regardless of race, gender, or socioeconomic background. This requires careful design and the use of unbiased data sets during training. 

If data reflects historical discrimination, AI can reproduce or even amplify those patterns. In hiring, for example, biased algorithms have favored male candidates over female ones with similar qualifications. In criminal justice, risk assessment tools have shown higher error rates for certain racial groups. 

Fairness in AI is essential for promoting equal opportunity and avoiding harm. Designing systems that recognize and correct for bias is a central part of ethical development.

Transparency and Explainability

People should be able to understand how and why an AI system made a specific decision. This transparency helps build trust between users, developers, and stakeholders. In sectors like healthcare or justice, decisions based on AI can have life-changing effects. 

An explainable and transparent AI model helps doctors understand diagnostic recommendations or allows judges to examine risk assessments in bail hearings. By contrast, when systems are too complex or hidden, accountability fades.

Ultimately, transparent design and clear documentation allow for scrutiny, corrections, and improvements. They also make it easier to identify and fix problems before they cause serious harm or lead to mistrust.

Accountability and Governance

When an AI system makes a mistake or causes harm, someone must be responsible. However, there is currently no federal legislation regulating the development of AI in the United States, which is a problem. Clear accountability ensures that creators, operators, or organizations do not shift blame to the technology.

Ethical AI development requires strong internal checks, routine audits, and clear reporting structures. External oversight, including regulatory bodies and legal frameworks, helps enforce these responsibilities. 

With well-defined governance, organizations can detect problems early, reduce risks, and respond more effectively when failures occur. In this sense, accountability is not only about legal responsibility but also about moral duty to ensure AI serves the public good and does not operate without human oversight.

Privacy and Data Protection

AI systems often rely on massive amounts of personal data, raising serious concerns about how that data is collected, stored, and used. Without strong privacy safeguards, individuals may face unwanted surveillance or identity exposure. 

Ethical AI respects consent, uses anonymization techniques, and includes strong data security measures. Facial recognition technologies, for example, though incredibly helpful in law enforcement, have been criticized for tracking people without their knowledge. In healthcare, AI tools that process patient data must protect sensitive information to maintain trust and comply with laws. 

Responsible design considers privacy from the beginning, not as an afterthought, because protecting personal data is a core part of ethical practice.

Human Rights and Safety

AI systems should support and protect basic human rights such as freedom, dignity, and equality. This involves inclusive design that considers the needs of people who are often overlooked, including those from marginalized communities. 

Ethical AI must also prioritize safety, both physical and psychological. Systems that make predictions, automate decisions, or interact with users must be tested for potential harm. 

Global frameworks like the OECD AI Principles and the UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI provide guidance on aligning AI with international human rights standards. As such, by respecting rights and promoting safety, AI becomes a force for social progress.

Common Ethical Issues in AI

Even with strong guiding principles, real-world ethical challenges still often emerge. These are not future concerns or theoretical debates. They are already impacting people through biased court algorithms, unfair hiring tools, and advanced surveillance systems. 

In sectors like justice, employment, and national security, the consequences of poor AI design or misuse are becoming increasingly visible. Identifying and addressing these issues is the first step toward building AI systems that are safer, more reliable, and aligned with public interest.

Algorithmic Bias and Discrimination

AI systems can produce unfair or harmful results when trained on biased data or built with flawed assumptions. For example, if past hiring records reflect racial or gender bias, an AI tool might learn to favor certain groups over others. 

One well-known case involved an AI hiring tool that prioritized resumes with White male names. This University of Washington study revealed that resumes with White-associated names were selected 85% of the time for moving forward to the next hiring stage, leaving resumes with Black-associated names at a 9% chance.

In another, predictive policing software led to over-policing in communities of color. The problem is that the data itself was already skewed. Over-policing in communities of color has resulted in more arrests in those areas, though not necessarily more crime.

These systems do not make decisions in a vacuum. They reflect the data they are given. Discriminatory outcomes not only harm individuals but can also damage public trust and expose organizations to legal and reputational risks.

Surveillance and Invasion of Privacy

Surveillance technologies that monitor people's faces, movements, and online activity are increasingly incorporating AI. In addition to facial recognition software, users may be monitored in ways that seem intrusive or opaque through location tracking applications and online behavior analysis. 

In the name of national security or business efficiency, these tools often operate with limited transparency. While some surveillance may help detect threats or improve services, it must be balanced against individual rights. 

Back in 2021, several cities around the world began banning or limiting facial recognition technologies due to privacy concerns. And though this technology still managed to make a comeback, such initiatives show the need for stricter rules and public debate on how AI should be used in monitoring people.

Job Displacement and Automation

AI-powered automation is rapidly changing the workforce, replacing human labor in roles ranging from manufacturing to customer service. While it can improve productivity and reduce costs, it also creates major ethical concerns. 

Many workers face job loss, skill gaps, and economic uncertainty, especially in sectors that rely on routine tasks. A Goldman Sachs report states AI could replace as many as 300 million full-time jobs. 

Addressing these effects calls for thoughtful policies. Governments and companies must invest in retraining programs, support systems, and long-term planning to help people transition into new roles. Ethical AI should not leave workers behind but instead consider their well-being during times of economic change.

Autonomous Weapons and Safety Risks

The development of AI-driven weapons, including drones and automated defense systems, introduces high-stakes ethical risks. Unlike traditional weapons, autonomous systems can identify and strike targets with minimal human input. 

This raises concerns about accountability, civilian harm, and the potential for malfunction or misuse. For example, if a self-guided drone misidentifies a target, who is responsible? The developer, the military, or the machine? 

Global organizations, like the UN, have called for bans or strict regulation on autonomous weapons due to fears of escalating conflict and reduced human control. As these technologies advance, safety and ethical oversight must remain at the center of global discussions.

Intellectual Property and Ai-Created Content

Traditional intellectual property laws were not designed for work created by machines. As AI tools generate music, paintings, written articles, and software code, questions about ownership and copyright are becoming harder to answer. 

One major challenge lies in defining authorship. If an AI produces a song or a design, is the credit due to the user, the developer, or the algorithm itself? There are also concerns about originality. Many AI models are trained on existing works, which can lead to outputs that resemble or reuse existing content. 

This creates a blurry line between creative inspiration and infringement. The issue has sparked debate in fields like journalism, education, and digital art, where content creation is essential. Regulators in some countries have started to explore policies for labeling AI-generated work or setting clear ownership rules. However, global standards remain inconsistent. As AI creativity grows, legal frameworks will need to evolve to balance innovation with protection.

Who Shapes AI Ethics?

As previously stated, there is no single voice that governs AI ethics. Numerous stakeholders, each with their own role, priorities, and influence, shape it. Together, they aid in defining the best ways to develop, implement, and regulate AI in order to uphold human values.

Key contributors include:

  • Governments: Create and enforce regulations that ensure AI systems meet public safety, privacy, and fairness standards. For example, the EU AI Act sets risk-based rules for AI use across Europe.
  • Academic institutions: Conduct research on ethical challenges, develop theoretical frameworks, and train the next generation of AI experts.
  • Tech companies: Build the AI systems and play a major role in setting internal ethical guidelines, conducting audits, and shaping best practices.
  • Ethicists: Provide moral and philosophical insights that challenge assumptions and promote responsible innovation.
  • International organizations: Coordinate global efforts, such as the OECD AI Principles, which promote inclusive, human-centered AI.

Collaboration among these groups is essential but not always easy. Tech companies often move faster than regulators, and power imbalances can lead to ethics being shaped more by corporate interests than public needs. Ensuring balanced input and transparent dialogue remains a major challenge in the future of AI ethics.

How to Build Ethical AI Systems

Creating ethical AI systems requires careful attention throughout every stage of development. It is not enough to focus solely on technical performance. Ethical considerations must be embedded from design through deployment and beyond. 

Here are the key steps to guide the process:

  • Conduct ethical audits regularly to evaluate AI systems for bias, fairness, and unintended consequences.
  • Form interdisciplinary teams that include ethicists, social scientists, and domain experts alongside engineers to provide diverse perspectives.
  • Practice inclusive design by involving users from different backgrounds to ensure AI works fairly for all communities.
  • Establish user feedback loops so real-world impacts are monitored and improvements made continuously.
  • Provide education and training in AI ethics for developers to raise awareness and build responsible habits.

Universities also play a crucial role in this effort. At the University of North Dakota (UND), we proudly integrate AI ethics into parts of our curriculum, preparing students to recognize ethical challenges and design technology that aligns with human values. 

By combining academic knowledge with practical experience, programs like the Master’s in Human-Technology Interaction and the Ph.D. in Artificial Intelligence at UND help shape a new generation of AI practitioners who prioritize ethical responsibility.

The Future of AI Ethics

In the future, AI ethics must keep pace with fast-moving technologies that bring new challenges. 

Emotional AI, designed to detect and respond to human emotions, raises concerns about privacy and manipulation, as highlighted by researchers at MIT who caution about the risks of misuse in sensitive contexts. Generative AI tools like GPT and DALL·E have sparked debates over copyright, misinformation, and authenticity.

Meanwhile, international regulation efforts are gaining momentum. The European Union’s proposed AI Act aims to set a global benchmark by categorizing AI risks and enforcing compliance. The United Nations, on the other hand, has also initiated discussions on AI governance frameworks to promote ethical standards worldwide.

Despite these efforts, however, countries vary in values and priorities. Some emphasize human rights and privacy, while others focus on economic growth and security. Global cooperation remains crucial to balance innovation with protection, ensuring AI serves humanity responsibly.

Conclusion

Ethical thinking should guide every step of AI development, from data collection to deployment. It helps ensure that AI systems support fairness, protect rights, and serve the public good. As technology continues to grow more powerful, the need for ethical guidance only becomes more urgent. 

The future of AI depends not only on what we build, but also on how thoughtfully we build it. What role will you play in shaping AI that reflects our best values, not our worst habits? At the University of North Dakota, you can gain the knowledge and tools to lead in the field of ethical AI.
