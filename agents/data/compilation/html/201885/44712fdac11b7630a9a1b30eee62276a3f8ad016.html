Limitations of AI Detection Tools

AI detection tools are not always accurate, and can be subject to false positives. Below are just a few resources discussing the limitations of current AI detection tools, and why you should exercise caution when using them to evaluate student work.

  • GPT detectors are biased against non-native English writers
    "GPT detectors frequently misclassify non-native English writing as AI generated, raising concerns about fairness and robustness."
  • AI wrote the US Constitution, says AI content detector
    Editorial explaining the limitations of AI detection tools.
  • AI Detection Is a Business. But Should It Be Faculty Business?
    Chronicle of Higher Education editorial that describes some of the dangers of using AI detectors for student work.
  • GenAI Detection Tools, Adversarial Techniques and Implications for Inclusivity in Higher Education
    "This study investigates the efficacy of six major Generative AI (GenAI) text detectors when confronted with machine-generated content that has been modified using techniques designed to evade detection by these tools."

AI in Medical Education

  • 5 Ways Medical Educators Can Use AI and Other Technologies
    This piece by Lisa Ellis from Harvard Medical School outlilnes five ways to use generative AI in Medical Education
  • AI: What do faculty need to know?
    This presentation by David Dorr at Oregon Health and Science University outlines the future of AI in medical education and the opportunity and challenges presented by the technology.
  • AMA Update: ChatGPT in medical education: Generative AI and the future of artificial intelligence in health care
    A discussion Dr. Marc Triola, associate dean of education informatics and director of the Institute for Innovations in Medical Education at NYU Grossman School of Medicine on how AI is changing medical education.
  • ChatGPT and Generative Artificial Intelligence for Medical Education: Potential Impact and Opportunity
    This article from Academic Medicine offers an overview of generative AI and offers insights into potential impacts and opportunities of the technology in medical education.
  • Artificial Intelligence for Health Professions Educators
    A discussion paper from the National Academy of Medicine on the use of artificial intelligence in health sciences education.
  • Medical students' AI literacy and attitudes towards AI: a cross-sectional two-center study using pre-validated assessment instruments
    An article from BMC Medical Education that investigates medical students' AI literacy and attitudes towards AI.
  • AI and Clinical Practice—Can AI Accelerate Medical Education?
    In this Q&A, JAMA Editor in Chief Kirsten Bibbins-Domingo and Bernard S. Chang, a neurologist and dean for medical education at Harvard Medical School, discuss how artificial intelligence will change medical education, admissions, and teaching the future generation of physicians and clinicians
