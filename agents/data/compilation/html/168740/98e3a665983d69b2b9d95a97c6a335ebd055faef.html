Welcome to Andrews University's AI Resource Hub

At Andrews University, we embrace the responsible use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) to enhance learning, research, and operations in ways that reflect our faith, values, and mission. Grounded in a biblical worldview and committed to innovation with integrity, this site provides guidance, approved tools, policies, and support to help faculty, staff, and students use AI ethically, effectively, and thoughtfully in their academic and professional journey.

Approved Generative AI Tools at Andrews University

Generative AI Tool Set Introduction

To support innovation while safeguarding data, Andrews University has curated a list of approved generative AI tools for use by faculty, staff, and students. This list reflects a thoughtful balance of security, compliance, educational value, and productivity enhancement.


Tools are categorized by their primary function, general productivity, data analysis, teaching and learning, or administrative automation and evaluated for their fit within higher education. Each tool includes guidance on acceptable data types. Use of AI tools must align with university policy, including data privacy, ethical integrity, and appropriate disclosure when used for academic or administrative work. This resource is designed to help our campus community use AI responsibly and effectively in support of our mission and values.


To ensure security, compliance, and responsible usage, the university categorizes AI tools based on their intended use: 

A. General Productivity AI (e.g., Chatbots, Writing Assistants): Can be used for non-sensitive tasks like drafting emails, summarizing content, and brainstorming ideas, provided no confidential data is shared.
B. AI for Data Analysis & Decision Support: Requires approval before implementation if it processes university data. Outputs must be reviewed for accuracy and bias.
C. AI in Teaching & Learning: Faculty and staff should refer to institutional guidelines on AI integration in coursework, assessments, and student support tools.
D. AI for Administrative Automation: Any AI handling student records, financial data, or personnel information must undergo a risk and compliance review before adoption. 

General AI Tools

Name Comments Types of Data Allowed
Adobe Firefly Strong visual tool for digital/media courses, design support, and content ideation. Faculty and students should review copyright and ethical usage guidelines for generated images. A
ChatGPT Free Excellent for early-stage idea generation, summaries, and informal tutoring. Should not be used with any confidential or sensitive data. Lacks access to newer GPT-4 capabilities. A
Claude Offers context-aware, thoughtful responses and is useful for long-form content analysis. Does not currently support integration with enterprise systems. A
Microsoft CoPilot Deep integration with Microsoft 365 makes this ideal for administrative and academic productivity (e.g., writing, summarizing, task management). Access controlled by institutional credentials. A, B, C, D
Grammarly Enhances academic and administrative writing quality. Institutional license includes advanced tone, clarity, and plagiarism checks. A
Zoom AI Companion Useful for automatic meeting transcriptions, summaries, and action items. Ensure settings prevent capture of sensitive information during private discussions. A
Google Gemini May offer benefits with content generation and summarization if governance is established. Not approved for sensitive data. A, B, C
EndNote25 Supports research citation management and integrates with academic databases. Fully supported by the university library and recommended for scholarly work. A

 

Teaching-Specific AI Tools

Name Comments Types of Data Allowed

AI Teaching Assistant Pro Assists with creating assessments, feedback, and rubrics. May enhance instructional efficiency but should be reviewed for academic integrity. A, C

Exploring Generative AI in Teaching & Learning

As generative AI tools rapidly evolve, so does our approach to navigating their impact in higher education. This curated resource from the Department of Digital Learning and Instructional Technology (DLIT) supports Andrews University faculty, staff, and students in exploring the responsible use of AI in academic environments.

From example syllabi language and classroom strategies to hands-on workshops, ethical considerations, and assignment design tips, this page provides practical guidance grounded in real campus conversations and experimentation. Whether you're curious, cautious, or somewhere in between, you'll find resources to help you teach, learn, and lead with confidence.

AI Learning Lab Sessions

AI Tools that Save Time
Discover practical AI tools that can streamline your daily work and academic tasks. Perfect for beginners, this session covers using Microsoft CoPilot within Andrews' AI policy guidelines.

Meeting Magic: Quick AI Hacks for Better Collaboration
Five quick techniques to make your meetings more productive using AI tools for agenda creation, note-taking, follow-up tasks, and action item tracking. This session focuses on Microsoft CoPilot and Zoom.

Talking to AI: The Art of Getting Better Results
Learn effective communication strategies with AI systems within appropriate professional and academic contexts. We'll cover prompt structure, context setting, and refinement techniques that improve AI interactions.

Assessment Makeover for AI Resistance
Three practical examples of adapting traditional assessments for an AI-present world. Each example showcased in 5 minutes with actionable takeaways for immediate implementation.

Teaching Enhanced: Integrating AI in the Classroom 
Explore evidence-based approaches to incorporating AI into your teaching practice. We'll showcase successful classroom implementations, discuss pedagogical frameworks, and share practical examples of AI-enhanced lesson planning, content creation, and student engagement. Learn how to leverage AI while maintaining your teaching philosophy and student learning outcomes.

AI Safety Check: Bias and Privacy Essentials
Essential awareness of AI limitations, bias recognition, and privacy protection strategies. Quick guidelines for responsible AI use within institutional policies and course-specific requirements. Emphasis on understanding appropriate contexts for AI use.

AI Creativity Unleashed: Visual Content Creation
 Explore AI tools for creating images, graphics, and creative content for presentations, marketing materials, and educational resources. Includes guidance on appropriate use contexts, ethical considerations, and quality standards.

Using AI Tools Responsibly in Research
 Three specialized AI tools for academic research presented in 5-minute deep dives. Focus on literature review, data analysis, and citation management applications.

Microlearning: Classroom Innovations: Three AI Teaching Enhancers
Three AI tools specifically designed for educational use, demonstrated with real classroom examples. Each tool presented in 5 minutes with immediate application ideas.

AI Learning Lab: Resistance is Not Futile: When and How to Teach an AI-Resistant Course
Learn to design courses emphasizing uniquely human skills like reflection, collaboration, and creativity that AI cannot replicate. Discover how to explain the educational value to students and why certain learning requires human-only practice. Explore smart assessment design that reveals authentic understanding rather than focusing on AI detection.
