UConn Cookie Information

Web cookies (also called HTTP cookies, browser cookies, or simply cookies) are small pieces of data that websites store on your device (computer, phone, etc.) through your web browser. They are used to remember information about you and your interactions with the site.

Purpose of Cookies:

  1. Session Management:

    • Keeping you logged in

    • Remembering items in a shopping cart

    • Saving language or theme preferences

  2. Personalization:

    • Tailoring content or ads based on your previous activity

  3. Tracking & Analytics:

    • Monitoring browsing behavior for analytics or marketing purposes

Types of Cookies:

  1. Session Cookies:

    • Temporary; deleted when you close your browser

    • Used for things like keeping you logged in during a single session

  2. Persistent Cookies:

    • Stored on your device until they expire or are manually deleted

    • Used for remembering login credentials, settings, etc.

  3. First-Party Cookies:

    • Set by the website you're visiting directly

  4. Third-Party Cookies:

    • Set by other domains (usually advertisers) embedded in the website

    • Commonly used for tracking across multiple sites

Authentication cookies are a special type of web cookie used to identify and verify a user after they log in to a website or web application.

What They Do:

Once you log in to a site, the server creates an authentication cookie and sends it to your browser. This cookie:

  • Proves to the website that you're logged in

  • Prevents you from having to log in again on every page you visit

  • Can persist across sessions if you select "Remember me"

What's Inside an Authentication Cookie?

Typically, it contains:

  • A unique session ID (not your actual password)

  • Optional metadata (e.g., expiration time, security flags)

Analytics cookies are cookies used to collect data about how visitors interact with a website. Their primary purpose is to help website owners understand and improve user experience by analyzing things like:

  • How users navigate the site

  • Which pages are most/least visited

  • How long users stay on each page

  • What device, browser, or location the user is from

What They Track:

Some examples of data analytics cookies may collect:

  • Page views and time spent on pages

  • Click paths (how users move from page to page)

  • Bounce rate (users who leave without interacting)

  • User demographics (location, language, device)

  • Referring websites (how users arrived at the site)

Heres how you can disable cookies in common browsers:

1. Google Chrome

  • Open Chrome and click the three vertical dots in the top-right corner.

  • Go to Settings > Privacy and security > Cookies and other site data.

  • Choose your preferred option:

    • Block all cookies (not recommended, can break most websites).

    • Block third-party cookies (can block ads and tracking cookies).

2. Mozilla Firefox

  • Open Firefox and click the three horizontal lines in the top-right corner.

  • Go to Settings > Privacy & Security.

  • Under the Enhanced Tracking Protection section, choose Strict to block most cookies or Custom to manually choose which cookies to block.

3. Safari

  • Open Safari and click Safari in the top-left corner of the screen.

  • Go to Preferences > Privacy.

  • Check Block all cookies to stop all cookies, or select options to block third-party cookies.

4. Microsoft Edge

  • Open Edge and click the three horizontal dots in the top-right corner.

  • Go to Settings > Privacy, search, and services > Cookies and site permissions.

  • Select your cookie settings from there, including blocking all cookies or blocking third-party cookies.

5. On Mobile (iOS/Android)

  • For Safari on iOS: Go to Settings > Safari > Privacy & Security > Block All Cookies.

  • For Chrome on Android: Open the app, tap the three dots, go to Settings > Privacy and security > Cookies.

Be Aware:

Disabling cookies can make your online experience more difficult. Some websites may not load properly, or you may be logged out frequently. Also, certain features may not work as expected.

What is generative AI

Generative AI is a technology for conversational information searches that can create original contentsuch as text, images, video, audio or software codein response to a users prompt or request (IBM, 2024) and which learns to generate more objects that look like the data it was trained on. (Zewe, 2023) In everyday usage, GenAI typically refers to chatbot interfaces trained on large bodies of data and algorithmic models, which can create natural language responses and images. Examples include Copilot, ChatGPT, Gemini, Llama, Claude, Midjourney, Dall-E, and others. GPT stands for General Pre-trained Transformer, a reference to an algorithm developed in the late 2010s using neural network type processing to increase the accuracy and speed of generated texts and images.

Do many students use it?

Both students and instructors increasingly use AI as part of teaching or learning. A study from 2024 showed that 30 percent of students reported using generative AI and more than fifty percent reported positive attitudes toward AI. There is confusion, however, about which professors allow AI to be used in projects, and where the boundary lies between appropriate collaboration and cheating. For these reasons, students may either avoid using AI entirely or use it inappropriately.

What should my policy on AI be?

While the university does not have a blanket AI policy, AI use is governed by the Data Classification Policy, the Academic, Scholarly, and Professional Integrity and Misconduct Policy, and the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act of 1974 (FERPA). Instructors are encouraged to put an AI policy on their syllabi. AI policies typically range from banning the use of AI to fully integrating it into every aspect of the course. Most courses adopt policy language somewhere in the middle.

It is important to remember that generative AI tools range from simple autofill in emails, through paraphrasing tools like Grammarly, to highly sophisticated generative chatbots like ChatGPT. We recommend your syllabus statement include information on at least the following details:

  • Which tools are okay to use?
  • How do you plan to hold students accountable for AI use?
  • Why do you have the policy you have?

How can I build student AI literacy and fluencies in my class?

Including AI or addressing it with your students will help prepare them for jobs or further work in most disciplines, in addition to building important technological literacy more broadly. AI use varies by discipline, profession, or enterprise. Please reach out to CETL for 1:1 consultations on integrating AI into activities and assignments in alignment with your course objectives.

The foundations of AI literacy are:

  • Knowing when to use AI and when not to
  • Knowing how to ask and refine questions with an AI chatbot (prompting)
  • Critically evaluating AI output
  • Conceptual understanding of generative AI models and algorithms
  • Identification of a wide range of risks in AI and AI enterprises

Is there any evidence that AI helps students learn?

Early 2023 marked the arrival of ChatGPT 3.5 and with it, a turning point in generative AI. Since then, research on generative AI and learning has exploded and continues to grow. Studies generally fall into two broad categories: 1) what students need to know about AI, and 2) unique affordances of AI for learning. This section focuses on the latter.

To date, studies of the unique affordances of AI for learning focus on learning outcomes such as:

  • Outcomes related to personalization in support of learning
  • Outcomes for creativity
  • Outcomes for critical thinking
  • Outcomes related to self-efficacy & motivation
  • Outcomes for second language learning

It is challenging to operationalize a multi-dimensional concept like learning. For this reason, there is currently no reliable consensus on AIs effects on learning. Whether that is unique to AI or applies across teaching methods and technologies is an open question contingent on many contextual factors. For example, in a comparison of digital technologies, Cubillos et al (2025) found that students using generative AI reported enhanced feelings of autonomy and choice, which can benefit learning, while students using videos to learn reported greater sense of competence. One meta-analysis (Sun & Zhou, 2024) showed a greater effect size for independent learning with AI than for collaborative learning, across disciplines; however, it is not clear whether this captures student preferences rather than an effect of instructional design. These are just two examples of the complexity of studying AIs impact on learning.

What are some AI tools I can use in my teaching?

UConn has a contract with Microsoft for basic access to Copilot. It is free to faculty, staff, and students. Enhanced subscriptions can also be purchased by departments. In both cases, the contract affords privacy protections and includes an agreement that queries will not be used to train large language models. Due to these protections, it is recommended, though not required, that you use Copilot.

This industry changes constantly, with new tools being added, enhanced, or removed.

The other most prominent chatbots include ChatGPT (owned by OpenAI), Llama (owned by Meta), Claude (owned by Anthropic), and Google Gemini (owned by Alphabet). Each has its own algorithms and configuration of data sets on which the model is trained. For image generation, Midjourney and DALL-E (and SORA for video) currently dominate. With a paid ChatGPT account, you can access custom GPTs (models) for, e.g., data analysis, diagnostics, etc., or create your own GPT through detailed instructions. Copilot does not feature custom GPT creation, but there are ways to instruct it that come close, especially in Copilot Plus (paid). If you are interested in doing this, you may find this Custom GPT Guide helpful.

When deciding on a tool and working with it, follow these practices:

  • Understand FERPA and follow UConns Data Classification Policy
  • Connect the choice to your course learning objectives
  • Transparently communicate to students your definition of plagiarism and cheating
  • Communicate to students the risks of AI
  • Require students to demonstrate comprehension of what is identifiable information so they can avoid entering it into an AI tool

To learn more about other GenAI tools, you might consult this Generative AI Product Tracker, maintained by Ithaka S/R.

Should I use detection software?

Currently detection software is of limited use and may be detrimental because it generates false negatives and, more importantly, false positives. Still, this technology continues to develop and is an important part of the AI landscape. For example, methods for protecting artists work and copyright involve detecting or in some cases distorting or blocking a file to prevent it from training image generation models like DALL E and Midjourney. Similarly, scholars are attentive to the need for transparency in what publishing companies do with their articles and books, e.g., selling them to AI companies. While this is more a copyright than detection issue, it points to a node of issues related to fair labor and academic integrity. The United States Copyright Office published guidance on copyright in January 2025.

What does generative AI do to assessment of learning?

While safeguarding the integrity of assessment practices is critical, it is equally important to recognize that cheating and plagiarism often stem from reasons other than lack of ethics. For example, students may not know what acceptable collaboration looks like; acceptable collaboration may differ from instructor to instructor, or tool to tool; students may be overwhelmed by the course material or other priorities in their lives such as jobs or family duties. There is a lot we can do, pedagogically, to scaffold assignments, make content accessible, and communicate the value and purpose of using generative AI critically and judiciously.

UConns University Senate, and the Office of the Provost, maintain guidelines for academic integrity and scholastic standards. The UConn Office of Community Standards offers information on handling plagiarism and cheating.

How are UConn instructors using AI in teaching?

Getting practice and gaining familiarity with AI tools is the best way to learn about their benefits in different contexts and for different tasks. AI tools will even tell you what the risks of AI are, if you ask it to. Well-known risks include loss of critical thinking, privacy violations, copyright violations, harm to labor in the global South, environmental impact on water and energy us, and even addiction to using AI chatbots. Additional Resources

  • AI in Education Resource Directory This google doc is maintained by the POD Network, a professional society of educational developers in higher education.
