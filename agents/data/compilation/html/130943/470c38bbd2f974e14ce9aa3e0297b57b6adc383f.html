Not All Artificial Intelligence Models are Created Equally: Privacy Challenges for Users of Generative Artificial Intelligence

Jan 28, 2025 | Newsletter

by PC Shea, UD Privacy Officer

More and more products are building generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) tools into their offerings. GenAI includes any machine-based tool designed to consider user questions, prompts, and other inputs (such as text, images, and videos) and create new data or content based on existing information available to them. These tools may be publicly available, private, standalone, or offered as components of other software. They are being incorporated into products we use every day including our phones, computers, and cars. Just recently Apple iOS and Google Gemini incorporated into their software updates significant new GenAI features and enhancements designed to improve productivity, learning, and overall user experience.

While GenAI tools can save time, help answer questions, and provide a boost in thinking about problems and resolutions to them, users must appreciate the privacy risk inherent with their use, especially the possibility of exposing sensitive, confidential information publicly. Information shared with GenAI tools may be used to teach the tools, called “training,” and will be available to other users of the tool. If the tool is public-facing, providing confidential or sensitive data to the tool will make it available publicly. If the GenAI tool is private, it still uses the information to train the tool, but it limits who can have access to the information to only certain authorized users. Knowing whether the tools are public or private is essential to avoid unintentionally revealing information that is private, subject to a contractual restriction, or legally prohibited from being disclosed.

Before using any publicly available GenAI tool, users should carefully examine the associated terms and conditions. The terms and conditions are a binding contract between the vendor and the users. Typically, the terms and conditions are written in very small print, are very long with lots of legalese, and users, unfortunately, typically agree to them without reading them. Failing to understand these ground rules regarding how the vendor can further use or disclose data easily leads to inadvertent and unintended disclosure of personal, confidential information. 

The University is cautiously moving to harness the power that GenAI tools provide given the privacy and other risks they present (e.g., inaccurate output). To minimize these risks and ensure appropriate use of GenAI tools at the University when using University assets (including University data), UD has developed a “Directive for the Use of Generative Artificial Intelligence” (the “Directive”). The Directive permits users of University assets (hardware, software, University information) to work with publicly available GenAI models if the only information being provided to the tools is public information. For example, if the University places the information on public-facing web pages (e.g., the University address), using the tools is permitted.

The Directive prohibits using public GenAI tools with non-public University Information (e.g., confidential, sensitive information). University Information includes information that the University uses to support its administration and missions, including research. Non-public University Information may only be used with GenAI tools that have been vetted and approved for use at the University. As a part of the vetting process, the University enters into an agreement with the vendor offering the tools that binds them contractually to maintaining the confidentiality of the information, limiting users of the tool to authorized University users, adopting appropriate security measures, and holding them accountable for any misuse of the information, among other things. 

A list of vetted GenAI tools is available at https://services.udel.edu/TDClient/32/Portal/KB/ArticleDet?ID=1150. The Directive also explains how to request additional GenAI tools to be vetted and possibly added to the approved list.

FAQs regarding the Directive are available at https://services.udel.edu/TDClient/32/Portal/KB/ArticleDet?ID=1149.
