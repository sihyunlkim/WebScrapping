Artificial Intelligence (AI) Usage Guidelines

  1. Information Security Home
  2. Information Hub
  3. Artificial Intelligence (AI) Usage Guidelines

Introduction

For these cybersecurity guidelines, AI refers to software algorithms capable of generating content, such as artwork, video, music, or text, based on input parameters or data (aka. prompts). These guidelines are subject to periodic review and updates, as necessary, to ensure they remain aligned with evolving technology and policies. These guidelines are published by the Information Security Office (ISO) in partnership with the Office of Information Technology (OIT).

Objective

Emerging generative AI tools including Microsoft Copilot, OpenAI ChatGPT, and Google Gemini can serve as powerful assets in supporting the work of organizations and individuals, yet they introduce unique challenges and ethical dilemmas. Use of AI tools could impact the cybersecurity of The University of Texas at Dallas (UT Dallas) in both positive and negative ways, suggesting the need for clear governance and documentation of expectations. Thus, these guidelines promote safe usage of generative AI tools and should be referenced when evaluating how best to manage new AI tools at UT Dallas.

Scope

These guidelines are intended primarily for UT Dallas employees performing official work duties. However, all campus community members may find the guidelines useful. The guidelines are not intended to govern academic conduct, plagiarism, or the impact of generative AI tools in the context of students in academic settings.

To better understand university policies related to academic work, please refer to UTDSP5017.

Risks of Using AI Tools

  • Users should be aware that the security of University Data may be undermined if processed by AI tools.
  • Users should be aware that output from AI tools is not guaranteed to be accurate, because AI learning models may produce output that is subject to biases, inaccuracies, or which may infringe on copyright laws.

Always Use UT Dallas Approved AI Tools First

  • When performing official UT Dallas job duties, it is recommended that employees use AI tools and generative AI software-as-a-service (Saas) tools offered by UT Dallas, rather than obtaining personal access to public or vended AI platforms because these are not approved and have not yet been evaluated for safety. Please note, AI Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) tools and sometimes referred to as AI as a Service (AIaas). Generally, these are tools hosted by another organization and offered via connection to the Internet.
  • At the time of this writing, OIT offers the following AI tools:
    • CometAI
    • Microsoft Copilot,
    • Amazon Bedrock
  • OIT can help UT Dallas employees by discussing needs and providing guidelines for safe handling of University Data when interacting with AI tools.
  • Prior to seeking additional AI service providers, employees should first consider the feasibility of existing service offerings contracted by UT Dallas, in consultation with OIT and Office of Contracts Administration (OCA).
  • Future procurement of third-party hosted solutions which offer AI features must be reviewed by OCA.

Acceptable Use of Other AI Tools

  • Users are allowed to input Public Data into generative AI tools.
  • AI tools may be used in the performance of research activities not classified as containing Confidential Data; Confidential Data should not be uploaded to AI tools unless such a tool is provided by UT Dallas.
  • Class instructors may use AI for developing instructional materials, provided that Confidential Data is not uploaded to AI tools unless provided by UT Dallas.
  • UT Dallas employees performing official work duties are accountable for deliberate and/or unintentional misuse of AI-generated content, dissemination of misinformation from AI-generated data, or exposure of Confidential Data.

Unacceptable Use of Other AI Tools

  • UT Dallas employees may have access to publicly available AI tools which they choose to utilize for personal reasons. Such tools, not officially offered by UT Dallas, may not be used in the performance of job duties, and may not receive Confidential Data, as defined by UTDBP3096 Information Security and Acceptable Use Policy.
  • Publicly available AI tools not officially offered by UT Dallas should not be used to produce data output that would then be classified as official University Data. Examples include generating data for non-public research, academic work, or instructional materials.

Special Considerations When Using AI Tools

  • Regulations
    • Actions performed with AI tools during the performance of job duties cannot violate any applicable state or federal law, UT Dallas policy, or UT System Administration policy.
    • The University Attorney is responsible for reviewing and approving specific scenarios where regulated data may, or may not, be allowed to be used with AI tools. Examples of regulated information that should not be input into AI tools include, but are not limited to:
      • Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) regulated data.
      • Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) regulated data.
  • Contractual and Licensing Requirements
    • Users must adhere to contractual and licensing terms associated with AI tools.
    • Datasets accessed through AI tools must be used in accordance with applicable contractual and licensing terms.
    • Data may be subject to contractual requirements that prohibit input of such data into AI tools, regardless of whether UT Dallas has approved and offered the AI tool.

Additional Considerations When Using AI Tools

  • Automation
    • UT Dallas employees should demonstrate thoughtfulness and responsibilities when considering opportunities for AI tools to fully automate university processes and functions. Such process changes must be considered:
      • Impact on current employees.
      • Applicable laws, contracts, and policies. For example, on June 22, 2025, the Texas governor signed the Texas Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act (TRAIGA or the Act) into law, making Texas the second state to pass comprehensive artificial intelligence (AI) regulation (with Colorado). 2.4.1.3 Potential for automation to produce outcomes that would be undesirable and unlikely to be produced by humans performing the process manually.
      • Potential for automation to produce outcomes that would be undesirable and unlikely to be produced by humans performing the process manually.
  • Audit Logging
    • It is preferrable that AI tools produce transaction logs and/or offer sufficient records to document the decisions made by such tools.
    • Users are reminded that University-owned networks and computing devices are subject to various forms of monitoring by organizational units, such as ISO and OIT, for the purpose of achieving intended performance and security protections.
  • Training
    • When UT Dallas contracts for AI tools, it is preferable to include specific and accepted training tools to familiarize employees with responsible usage.
  • End of Contract
    • When UT Dallas contracts for AI tools, it is preferable to include specific and acceptable methods of data destruction to be followed at the end of the business arrangement to ensure protection of University Data.
  • Oversight
    • It is preferable that impactful decisions made by AI tools be reviewed and approved by human employees with sufficient knowledge of the relevant processes, stakeholders, and other considerations, thus promoting the reasonableness and soundness of decisions.

Ethical Use of Generative AI

  • Human Rights and Privacy
    • Generative AI tools should be used in a manner that upholds and respects human rights and privacy. Content generated should not be discriminatory, harassing, or defamatory.
  • Bias and Discrimination
    • Users must understand that AI learning models receive incomplete or biased data as inputs, and as a result, may output imperfect data. UT Dallas employees should strive to avoid perpetuating biases through AI. This includes biases related to race, gender, age, and other protected characteristics.
  • Transparency and Accountability
    • Users of AI tools are encouraged to be transparent about the use of AI-generated content and will be held responsible for any content they create with AI and subsequently portray as their own original work.
  • Harm to Individuals or Groups
    • Content generated through AI should not cause harm to individuals, groups, or communities, and should comply with applicable laws and regulations.
  • Positive and Constructive Content
    • Users should strive to generate positive, constructive content aligned with the Universitys values, mission, and policies.

Incident Response and Reporting

  • Please contact infosecurity@utdallas.edu with questions, comments, and/or concerns.

Consultation

In the event that an employee of UT Dallas believes they might not be able to align their work within the boundaries offered in these guidelines, please contact infosecurity@utdallas.edu for consultation.

Conclusion

  • AI tools can enhance teaching, learning, and research at The University of Texas at Dallas, but it is important to approach usage with caution and responsibility. By adhering to these guidelines, the campus community can support the Universitys mission while minimizing risk.
