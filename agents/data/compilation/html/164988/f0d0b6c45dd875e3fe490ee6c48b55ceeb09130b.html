Getting Started

Generative AI tools create new content - text, images, code, music - by learning patterns from large datasets. Unlike search engines, they generate original responses, which can sometimes be inaccurate. Common tools include ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, and DALL·E. Always verify AI‑generated information and cite it appropriately in academic work.

These tools can support writing, learning, and brainstorming by helping you draft ideas, clarify concepts, and explore research directions. They work best when you give clear instructions and apply your own critical judgment. Think of AI as a helpful but imperfect assistant.

A quick note on research: while AI tools and Google can be useful for exploring early ideas, the most reliable starting point for academic work is library databases. They provide peer‑reviewed, authoritative sources that AI cannot access or replace. AI is great for brainstorming, generating keywords, and getting unstuck, but it’s not dependable for actual research because it can’t search library resources, may invent information, and often produces citations or “facts” that can’t be verified.

BU‑Supported AI Tools
Many tools are free with account registration. BU also provides access to:

  • Terrier GPT
    Secure platform offering BU users access to OpenAI, Claude, and Meta models.
  • Microsoft Copilot
    Drafting, editing, and data‑analysis support via BU Office 365.
  • Google Gemini
    AI chatbot integrated with BU’s Google Workspace.
  • NotebookLM
    Basic access included with Gemini (some features limited by licensing).
  • Adobe Creative Cloud
    AI for image generation, content editing, and video work.
  • GitHub Copilot
    Coding assistant free for BU students and faculty.
  • Zoom AI Companion
    Meeting summaries, action items, and automated Q&A.


Explore additional sources through the BU AI Development Accelerator.
