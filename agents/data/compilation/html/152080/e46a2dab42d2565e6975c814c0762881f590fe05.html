In Case You Missed It: Workshop Explores AI and Academic Integrity on Written Assignments

Published: March 12, 2025

Author: ND Learning

Earlier this month, Notre Dame Learning’s Kaneb Center for Teaching Excellence and the University’s Office of Academic Standards hosted a workshop on “Academic Integrity on Written Assignments: Surveys and Solutions,” bringing faculty together to explore student writing in the context of the challenges and opportunities posed by generative AI.

The session was led by Alex Ambrose and James Lang, both professors of the practice in the Kaneb Center, and Ardea Russo, director of the Office of Academic Standards. It offered a mix of research insights, practical strategies, and pedagogical and philosophical discussions for maintaining academic integrity in the age of AI-assisted writing.

Key Takeaways

Faculty are Grappling with AI-Generated Writing

The workshop presented two real-world scenarios—one where a student submitted a personal essay with no personal voice and suspiciously sophisticated language, and another where an international student’s writing suddenly appeared highly polished. Faculty discussed their responses, from direct conversations with students to rethinking assignment design to discourage over-reliance on AI tools.

Student Use of AI is Widespread, But Many are Unsure About Policies

Recent surveys conducted in Notre Dame’s Writing & Rhetoric courses revealed that about two-thirds of students never use AI when it’s not permitted, but a significant portion remain unsure about the boundaries of acceptable use. Faculty guidance on AI use varies widely, with only seven percent of students reporting that all their instructors provided clear AI policies.

AI Violations are on the Rise—But Not Just in Writing Courses

Data from the Office of Academic Standards showed that AI-related Honor Code violations have steadily increased. While most cases occur in Arts and Letters courses, unauthorized AI use has also been reported in business and science assignments, including capstone projects and in-class exams.

Detection is Tricky—Prevention is Better

Faculty shared challenges with AI detection tools, which often produce unreliable results. Workshop presenters outlined more substantive methods of shifting our assessment strategies, including “next-generation” assessments such as requiring students to submit an assignment appendix detailing their AI use. Instructors might also integrate oral reflections, in-class writing exercises, and creative statement components to verify authentic student engagement.

Students Need Clear, Transparent Policies

A key theme of the session was the importance of explicit AI policies at both the course and assignment levels. The workshop introduced frameworks like the Generative AI Acceptable Use Scale (a “traffic light” model for AI permissions) and the AI Menu (a more nuanced guide to what AI use is acceptable for different writing tasks). See our related resource article on “Enhancing Assignments with AI Transparency.”

What’s Next?

As AI continues to evolve, faculty play a critical role in shaping responsible student use. The workshop underscored the need for ongoing dialogue, clear communication, and thoughtful assignment design to uphold academic integrity while acknowledging the realities of AI in student learning.
