For Students: May I use Generative AI?

Should you be using Generative AI, even if it is properly credited?

 In many teaching, research, and publication situations, legal or policy constraints forbid or restrict the use of generative AI. 

For Class (Students)

  • VCU's Gen AI Guidelines for Students
    This page offers general guidelines, including specific information for graduate students. In a particular course, pay attention to the syllabus and the requirements of the specific assignment or assessment to know what is permissible or not permissible and ask questions if you are uncertain.

 

 

For Faculty: May I use Generative AI?

Should you be using Generative AI, even if it is properly credited?

 In many teaching, research, and publication situations, legal or policy constraints forbid or restrict the use of generative AI. 

All VCU Employees

  • VCU Policy on Acceptable Use of Generative AI Technology (for employees)
    Covers a variety of issues related to generative AI, with principles of ethical use, compliance, transparency, and accountability. Importantly, "Only GenAI tools that have been vetted and approved through the University IT Governance Process may be used within the university for sensitive and non-public (Category II) and confidential and regulated (Category I) data."

For Class (Faculty)

  • VCU Gen AI and Teaching Guidelines - Faculty
    Includes tips for syllabus language and for incorporating generative AI into instruction, as well as the warning, "In addition to student information (such as email addresses) all work submitted by students is protected under FERPA, and therefore cannot be uploaded to a Gen AI tool without their written consent. (This includes Gen AI detection tools, unless VCU has a contract with the provider of that particular detection tool.) Students, however, can upload their own graded materials into such tools. This, however, is not recommended unless VCU has a contract with a Gen AI tool’s provider, specifying that the submitted data will be protected.
  • VCU CIO Generative AI Guidelines
    The important part of these guidelines for teaching, especially grading, is the section on confidentiality, which states, "Do not enter confidential (Category 1) or sensitive (Category 2) data into any AI tools unless they have been approved for use by VCU IT Governance." and includes student names, IDs, email addresses, records, and grades as confidential or sensitive information.

For Writing in Academic Journals

Each publisher and sometimes even each publication venue for the same publisher will have its own policy on the use of generative AI. It is especially important to check the instructions for authors to understand the details of a particular venue's policy regarding how much use of generative AI is permissible and how its use should be documented.

  • COPE Position on Authorship and AI Tools
    While each publisher creates its own guidance for authors and editors, in 2023, Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE) laid out principles that many publishers follow. 
    In summary, those principles are
    * AI Tools should not receive authorship credit.
    * The use of generative AI should be clearly declared and described.
    * The authors are ultimately for responsible conduct of research, including ethical use of AI and the accuracy of the text of the publication.
  • International Committee of Medical Journal Editors Recommendations for the Conduct, Reporting, Editing, and Publication of Scholarly Work in Medical Journals
    As of January 2025, generative AI in writing was covered in section 2.A.4 with guidance similar to COPE's.

In Scholarly Peer Review

As above, the expectations will depend on the particular circumstance, so it is important to read and follow the instructions for reviewers in the particular situation.

Most peer review processes for publications and grants require reviewers to agree to keep the contents of the materials submitted for review confidential. Because providing submitted materials to a generative AI could result in a breach of confidentiality, using it is generally forbidden. Use of generative AI in refining the writing of peer reviews also may be forbidden, depending on the particular situation. 

  • Science funding agencies say no to using AI for peer review
    July 2023 article from the news section of Science, describing policies that were developing in government research agencies around the use of AI in peer review for grants
  • Where next in peer review? Part 2: COPE commentary
    Editorial column describing multiple issues involved in using AI for peer review, with both positive aspects, such as analytical AI successfully screening submissions for likelihood of being accepted, and negative aspects, such as threats to confidentiality if submissions become part of the training data for generative AI.
