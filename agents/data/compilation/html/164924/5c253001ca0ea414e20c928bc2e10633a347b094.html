Introduction

Artificial intelligence has been part of legal research for some time. AI allowed for the development of tools like natural language search, advanced citation and document analysis, and legal analytics tools. The introduction of generative artificial intelligence has rapidly increased the number of tools available to assist with legal research.

This research guide is designed to provide you with an overview of the new developments in artificial intelligence (AI) resources, including generative artificial intelligence, and their use in legal research. It will focus on AI tools designed for research, like Lexis Protégé and West CoCounsel. The guide will introduce these platforms, provide recommendations on how to effectively use them as part of your legal research process, and address important considerations involving their use in law school and in practice. 

What is Generative AI?

Generative AI is a tool that can create new content in response to a query from a user. These queries are called prompts. Generative AI tools are founded on large language models (LLMs) that have been trained on large sets of data and learn to recognize and predict patterns in that data. That learning is used to create novel content in response to user prompts. Generative AI can produce new text, images, and videos. (Generative Artificial Intelligence and Data Privacy: A Primer (2025), https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47569.)

General Purpose AI

General-purpose AI tools include Copilot, Gemini, and ChatGPT. These tools have been trained on datasets of publicly available content from the internet including books, articles, government data, and websites. They are able to generate text, summarize documents, brainstorm, translate, and create images. While general-purpose Gen AI can accurately perform many tasks, there is a greater risk of errors when performing high-stakes tasks requiring specialized knowledge. These errors are known as hallucinations.  

BC students, faculty, and staff have access to three general purpose AI tools with data protection. For more information on accessing these AI tools, visit the Boston College Generative AI website.

  • Microsoft Copilot Chatbot
  • Google Gemini Chatbot
  • Google NotebookLM

Legal Artificial Intelligence

The AI tools available on legal research platforms that we will focus on in this guide work a bit differently than general-purpose AI tools. They are trained on proprietary data sets of legal materials that are maintained and updated by legal publishers. Users can apply jurisdictional filters to a prompt to ensure responses are based on the proper controlling authority. Output includes citations to the legal authority upon which it is based. These characteristics of legal AI make these tools more accurate and reliable for conducting legal research. Legal AI tools can also perform other tasks such as drafting and analyzing legal documents. Another important feature of legal AI tools are their security and data privacy technology that allow client information to be secure and remain confidential as required by ethics rules.

BC Law students and faculty have access to the following legal  AI tools. For more information on these resources, see Legal Research AI Tools.

  • Lexis Protégé
  • West CoCounsel
  • Bloomberg Law AI Assistant

Comparison Chart

Comparison of Artificial Intelligence Tools

Feature General-Purpose AI Legal Generative AI
Training Data Huge, unedited dataset from internet sources. Smaller set of edited and verified legal materials owned and updated by legal publishers.
Citation and Source Information General-purpose AI tools can generate fictitious citations to legal authority in response to prompts.  Responses contain citations and links to authoritative legal documents within proprietary database allowing researcher to verify content of response.
Jurisdictional Focus User can include jurisdiction in a prompt but because general purpose AI is trained on a global data, responses may confuse legal rules and provide  incorrect information. User can limit a prompt to a specific jurisdiction in order to ensure proper legal authority and relevance.
Client Confidentiality While BC provides general AI tools with data protection, the University's Data Security Policy does not permit that any confidential information be used with these tools without University review. You should also check any similar policies in effect at any places of employment as similar restrictions may apply.  Contain high level security and guaranty that user data and prompts are not used to train the AI model in order to preserve client confidentiality.
