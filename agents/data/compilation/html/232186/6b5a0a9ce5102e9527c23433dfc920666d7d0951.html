AI and Personal Choice: Navigating the Decision to Opt Out

Your Right to Choose

While AI tools can offer benefits for work, research, and learning, choosing not to use them is a valid and respected decision. This page provides guidance for anyone at George Mason—students, faculty, staff, and researchers—who prefer not to use AI tools.

You can navigate environments where AI use is common while maintaining your personal, professional, or academic standards.

Key takeaways:

  • Your choice to avoid AI is valid and deserves respect
  • Traditional methods can lead to excellent outcomes and distinctive expertise
  • Support and resources are available to help you succeed with your chosen approach
  • Understanding AI developments can be valuable even without hands-on use
  • Your human-centered skills may become increasingly valuable as AI becomes more common

 

**This guide follows George Mason University's AI Guidelines and is regularly updated to reflect evolving best practices and policies. Claude.ai was used for formatting the information and content was reviewed by Dr. Heidi Blackburn.

Why People Choose Not to Use AI

Academic and Professional Development Concerns

  • Skill Development Focus Many people want to develop critical thinking, writing, and research skills without AI assistance. George Mason recognizes that "while AI can very quickly produce content for coursework, students should be aware that it could potentially come at the expense of deep learning and skills development."
  • Professional Integrity Some choose to complete all work independently as a matter of personal commitment.
  • Intellectual Ownership Others want to ensure all ideas and expressions are entirely their own creation.

Ethical and Philosophical Objections

  • Environmental Impact George Mason acknowledges that "every prompt provided to an AI system uses electricity and water" and encourages consideration of environmental costs.
  • Labor and Consent Issues Some object to AI systems trained on work created without consent from the original creators.
  • Bias and Fairness Concerns Others refuse to use systems that may perpetuate harmful biases against certain groups.
  • Data Privacy Concerns Many object to how AI companies collect and use personal information.

Religious or Cultural Considerations

Some people's faith-based principles emphasize authentic human expression and creativity. Cultural values may emphasize traditional learning and work methods. Community standards may prioritize human-generated work.

Practical and Personal Reasons

  • Privacy Protection Discomfort with data sharing and digital tracking.
  • Technology Approach Preference for established, proven methods over new technologies.
  • Economic Considerations Unwillingness to financially support certain AI business models.
  • Accessibility Needs AI tools may not work well with assistive technologies some users require.

Professional and Field-Specific Considerations

Some academic disciplines discourage AI use. Certain careers require skills developed without AI assistance. Building expertise that differentiates your work from AI-assisted output.

  • APA Psychology Teacher Network – Teaching academic integrity in the era of AI
    Educators are rethinking how to teach integrity as AI tools become more prevalent. The article offers strategies for reinforcing independent work and ethical boundaries in classrooms.
  • Harvard Business Review – Generative AI Has an Intellectual Property Problem
    This article examines how generative AI blurs the lines of authorship and originality, raising concerns about intellectual property rights. It warns that creators may lose control over their work as AI systems remix and reproduce content without attribution.
  • MIT News – Explained: Generative AI’s environmental impact
    MIT breaks down the environmental costs of generative AI, including high electricity consumption and water usage for cooling data centers. It urges users and developers to consider sustainability when deploying large-scale AI systems.
  • MIT Sloan – Bringing transparency to the data used to train artificial intelligence
    This article explores how AI models are trained on massive datasets often scraped from the internet without clear documentation or consent. It calls for greater transparency and accountability to protect creators and comply with emerging legal standards.
  • Harvard Business Review – Eliminating Algorithmic Bias Is Just the Beginning of Equitable AI
    HBR argues that addressing algorithmic bias is only the first step toward building equitable AI systems. The article highlights how AI can reinforce systemic inequalities unless organizations take proactive steps to ensure fairness across race, gender, and socioeconomic status.
  • AI and Faith – Religious Ethics in the Age of Artificial Intelligence and Robotics
    This article explores how various faith traditions approach AI through the lens of moral responsibility, creativity, and human dignity. It emphasizes the importance of authentic human expression and cautions against outsourcing spiritual and ethical decision-making to machines.
  • Proton – AI privacy concerns: How exposed is your personal data?
    Proton explains how AI systems collect and use personal data—even from users who never interact with them directly. It highlights the risks of digital tracking and the importance of privacy-first tools.
  • IEEE Computer Society – The Economics of AI: Disruptions, Challenges, and Opportunities
    This article discusses skepticism toward rapid AI adoption and preference for proven, human-centered methods. It also explores economic concerns about the sustainability and ethics of AI business models.
  • University of Washington – Can AI help boost accessibility? These researchers tested it for themselves
    Researchers found that while AI tools offer some accessibility benefits, they also perpetuate ableist biases and fail key usability tests. The article calls for inclusive design and better integration with assistive technologies.

George Mason University Policies on AI Choice

Your Rights at George Mason

Student Rights

  • Instructors cannot require AI use unless explicitly detailed in the course syllabus with clear justification
  • You have "the right to attend classes without being recorded by AI tools used by others"
  • You can seek clarification from instructors if AI policies are unclear
  • You can request alternative assignment approaches when AI use conflicts with your values

All Community Members

  • AI tool selection remains a personal choice within university policy boundaries
  • You can request alternatives to AI-required processes where feasible
  • Your choice to avoid AI should not disadvantage you professionally or academically

Balanced Approach to AI Literacy

George Mason recognizes that even people who avoid using AI need some understanding of the technology.

The university states: "Even if your inclination is to reject using AI for most of your classwork, you should nonetheless understand how to use it and how to leverage these systems at least to the level expected by employers."

Ways to develop AI literacy without hands-on use:

  • Read research about AI capabilities and limitations
  • Attend lectures about AI applications in your field
  • Study AI ethics and societal implications
  • Understand AI terminology and concepts through traditional learning methods

This knowledge helps you articulate your human-centered approach and understand workplace discussions about AI.

Navigating Academic Settings

When Instructors Encourage AI Use

Step-by-step approach:

  1. Review the syllabus carefully for specific AI policies and requirements
  2. Identify alternatives that meet the same learning objectives
  3. Schedule a conversation with your instructor early in the semester
  4. Propose specific alternatives rather than simply declining to participate
  5. Document the agreement for your records

Sample communication script: "I have concerns about using AI tools and would prefer to complete assignments using traditional methods. I've reviewed the syllabus and understand the learning objectives. Could we discuss alternative approaches that would demonstrate the same skills and knowledge?"

When AI Seems Expected But Not Required

Focus areas for success:

  • Develop skills in areas requiring human judgment and creativity
  • Excel in critical analysis and original thinking
  • Build expertise in traditional research methods
  • Strengthen collaboration and communication abilities

Practical strategies:

  • Start assignments earlier to allow time for traditional research methods
  • Use human networks for feedback and collaboration
  • Master traditional tools like citation managers and databases
  • Develop strong relationships with faculty and mentors

Navigating Professional and Research Settings

When Your Field Emphasizes AI

Professional development without AI use:

  1. Stay informed about AI developments through traditional learning methods
  2. Develop complementary skills that AI cannot replicate well
  3. Build expertise in areas requiring human insight and creativity
  4. Network with professionals who value traditional approaches
  5. Position your skills as valuable alternatives to AI-assisted work

Research Settings

Traditional research advantages:

  • Deep engagement with primary sources
  • Original methodological approaches
  • Human insight in data interpretation
  • Authentic relationship-building with research participants
  • Creative problem-solving without technological dependencies

Key strategies:

  • Partner with George Mason University librarians for comprehensive research support
  • Join research groups that value traditional methodologies
  • Develop expertise in specialized research techniques
  • Build networks of human collaborators and mentors

Managing Collaborative Work

Team Projects with AI Users

Successful collaboration steps:

  1. Discuss approaches early in the project planning phase
  2. Define clear roles that leverage each person's preferred methods
  3. Establish quality standards that work for all approaches
  4. Create documentation systems that track different types of contributions
  5. Regular check-ins to ensure all methods are contributing effectively

Communication strategies:

  • Focus on shared goals rather than methods
  • Emphasize the value different approaches bring
  • Be flexible about timelines that accommodate different working styles
  • Celebrate diverse contributions to team success

Professional Collaboration

In workplace settings:

  • Volunteer for projects that benefit from human-centered approaches
  • Offer traditional alternatives when teams face AI-related challenges
  • Share expertise in areas where human skills excel
  • Build reputation for reliability and authentic insights

Frequently Asked Questions

Rights and Requirements

Question: Can George Mason require me to use AI tools?

Answer: For students, AI requirements must be clearly stated in course syllabi with educational justification. Generally, you cannot be required to use AI unless it's explicitly necessary for specific learning objectives. For faculty and staff, consult with your department about alternative approaches to required tasks.

Question: What if my instructor assumes I'll use AI but doesn't explicitly require it?

Answer: Communicate early with your instructor about your preference. Most faculty will work with you to find alternative approaches that meet the same learning objectives.

Question: Do I need to understand AI even if I don't use it?

Answer: George Mason suggests that understanding AI systems "at least to the level expected by employers" is valuable even for non-users. You can develop this understanding through study and observation rather than hands-on use.

Question: How do I handle team projects when others want to use AI?

Answer: Establish clear agreements early about different approaches. Focus on ensuring your contributions are valued and that team goals are met through diverse methods. Document your specific contributions clearly.

Question: What if I change my mind later about using AI?

Answer: Your approach can evolve as you learn more and as circumstances change. The skills you develop through traditional methods will enhance any future AI use you might choose.

Question: Am I disadvantaging myself academically by not using AI?

Answer: Traditional methods often lead to deeper learning and skill development. Many academic assessments are designed to evaluate human thinking and creativity, where your approach may actually be advantageous.

Question: How do I prove I didn't use AI if questioned?

Answer: Keep good records of your research and writing process. Save drafts, research notes, and source materials. Document your methodology clearly.

Addressing Common Challenges

Responding to External Pressure

Common concerns you may hear:

  • "Everyone else uses AI, why don't you?"
  • "You'll be so much slower without AI help"
  • "You'll be disadvantaged professionally"
  • "You're just resisting technological progress"

Effective response strategies:

  1. Stay confident in your reasoning - your concerns are valid
  2. Focus on your values - remind yourself why this choice matters
  3. Highlight your unique strengths - emphasize skills you're developing
  4. Find supportive community - connect with others who share your approach
  5. Stay informed - understand AI developments without feeling pressured to adopt
