Generative AI in Education

The educational technology landscape is evolving quickly. Generative AI has enabled a large number of new technological capabilities that alter student learning and educational approaches. Across NYU’s global network there are a variety of resources for NYU Abu Dhabi faculty, instructors, and students as we adapt to AI-enabled tools.

Teaching and Learning resources from NYU’s Global Network are available on the following websites:

  • Teaching and Learning with AI
  • Teaching and Learning Services
  • Guidelines for Technology in Faculty Research

Video: Generative AI in the Classroom

Generative artificial intelligence, which includes natural language processing systems, may have disrupted some of the traditional classroom practices but it also offers new and exciting opportunities. The prevalence of generative AI demands a rethinking of traditional assessment methods as well as a call to come up with creative ways to use it for an enhanced learning experience. Watch this video to learn more about how you can adapt your teaching style to take Generative AI into account.

Openai’s ChatGPT4 (version launched March 14th, 2023) is a generative AI platform that produces text, code, and images (through Dall-e) from simple prompts. 

This new generative AI platform, and others like it are being used by students. It is a powerful new tool for generating sophisticated content. We need to respond to it in our courses, especially as the sophistication of the platform’s capabilities is constantly increasing.

Below are some suggestions of how to talk with your students about the rules and regulations of using this tool in your fall 2023 courses. You can get a free account at chat.openai.com.

How to talk with your students about the rules and regulations of using AI:

It is crucial to note that you should never enter personal student information into ChatGPT, nor your own. The platform is trained on what you enter into it and the content you enter in can be reproduced in a response to someone else’s prompt.**

We ask all faculty to include a ChatGPT statement on their syllabus and discuss this with your students. Please also ensure you have a notice on your syllabus that you reserve the right to use TurnItIn for written assignments.

To get started in reflection on how ChatGPT impacts your curriculum and pedagogy ask yourself these four questions:

  1. What are the cognitive tasks students need to perform without AI assistance? When should students rely on AI assistance from ChatGPT or similar platforms (Google’s Bard, Microsoft Bing, etc.)? 
  2. Where can an AI facilitate a better outcome? 
  3. Are there efficiencies in grading that can be gained? Are new rubrics and assignment descriptions needed? 
  4. Will you add a generative ai code of conduct to your syllabus?

Whatever strategy you choose to pursue, all strategies require explicit communications with your students about your expectations and your hopes for what they will learn from the assignments. Please include a description of your preference of when, how, and where ChatGPT can be used in your course on your syllabus and discuss it with your students.

What we'll be doing over the next few years will involve specific adaptations to the strengths and weaknesses of specific tools, but the underlying change in teaching and learning is moving faculty towards being more explicit about both goals and strategies to pursue those goals in their classrooms.

Clay Shirky, NYU Vice Provost for AI and Technology in Education

Optional Syllabus Language for ChatGPT Use

  • Sample statements for syllabi recommending against or forbidding the use of these tools. (The first two are taken from Advice Concerning the Increase in AI-Assisted Writing, PDF.)

    Sample Syllabus Language

    It is not permitted to use ChatGPT for any submitted and assessed assignments in this course. The use of AI software or apps to write or paraphrase text for your paper is not permitted. The use and submission of AI software or apps to write or paraphrase text for your paper constitutes fraud, as you did not author these words or ideas. You may not represent the output of an AI / machine-learning tool as your own work. This would be dishonest, and is a violation of the academic integrity codes of New York University. For instance, if an algorithm constructs an image, a block of code, or a paragraph in response to a prompt, it's dishonest to turn it in as your own creation without disclosing that fact.

    Faculty wishing to adopt this strategy will need to be explicit about the value you expect students to get out of any given assignment — what is the purpose of having them write and edit a document themselves when this is now automated? What do you think they will learn from the various stages of composition? How do generative tools interfere with those goals? What is your rationale for disallowing use of these tools? Note that there are currently no software sources able to reliably detect the use of generative AI.

  • Limiting the use of these tools requires explaining to your students where they are and are not appropriate. Perhaps you want to allow the use of these tools to brainstorm ideas, or to produce first drafts, but you expect the students to make substantial changes to the final product. Perhaps you want to allow use of these tools, so long as students include their prompts, and the unedited output, so they can demonstrate critical use of these tools.

    Like the strategy of resisting these tools altogether, this strategy requires enlisting students in the shared project of using generative AI in a relatively small number of approved ways.

    Sample Syllabus Language

    You are allowed to use ChatGPT to brainstorm and draft ideas in this class. The use of generative AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Dall-e, Codex, etc.) is permitted in this course for the following activities: Brainstorming and refining your ideas; Fine-tuning your research questions; Finding information on your topic; Drafting an outline to organize your thoughts; and Checking grammar and style. 

    The use of generative AI tools is not permitted in this course for the following activities: Impersonating you in classroom contexts, such as by using the tool to compose discussion board prompts assigned to you, reading summaries, or content that you put into a Zoom chat. Using ChatGPT to complete group work that your group has assigned to you is not allowed, unless it is mutually agreed upon that you may utilize the tool. It is not permitted to use ChatGPT to write a full draft of a writing assignment. Writing entire paragraphs or papers to complete class assignments with ChatGPT  is not appropriate nor permitted. TurnItIn can be used in this class.

  • Integrating the use of generative AI tools requires explaining to your students where these tools can help their thinking, and where you think these tools may end up being used in related professional practice. This is on a spectrum with specifying limited use of these tools, but with greater acceptance that any structured work you receive will be a hybrid of student and machine work. 

    Embracing these tools provides the greatest range of strategies. You could:

    • Have students use ChatGPT to write a response to their question or challenge.
    • Ask students to reflect on ChatGPT’s output (e.g., what is correct, incorrect, what they don’t know if it is correct or incorrect, what should they look up elsewhere to verify, what should they ask ChatGPT next).
    • Using Track Changes in MS Word or Suggesting in Google Docs, have students improve the output of ChatGPT (e.g., correcting errors or misinformation, expanding on shallow content). Have students submit their prompt and the improved ChatGPT response with their added content highlighted.
    • Ask students to individually identify a major question or challenge in your field or discipline that ChatGPT could write about. Have students use ChatGPT to write a response to their question or challenge.
    • Have students revise ChatGPT’s output from a different angle. For instance, take a different perspective, apply a critical lens, expand on a particular concept, or correct aspects of the output that could cause their peers to misunderstand or misinterpret.

    (Examples drawn from "Update Your Course Syllabus for ChatGPT.")

    Sample Syllabus Language

    Use of ChatGPT and related tools is allowed in this class, but only in ways noted in the assignments. When you use any of these tools, you must include a note describing how you used them with the assignment. Please consult the assignment descriptions to learn more about appropriate use of ChatGPT and other generative AI tools.

    (Source: much of the content here is developed by the NYU Informal Working Group on ChatGPT and Generative AI)

NotebookLM

NotebookLM is an AI-powered research assistant designed to help students and faculty synthesize information and engage with reading materials in new ways. Unlike ChatGPT, which generates output based on knowledge from pre-trained datasets, NotebookLM allows users to easily organize and manage course materials by uploading files (PDFs, websites, text, videos, or audio files) and generating responses based on the uploaded sources.

A key feature of NotebookLM is its citation system — whenever it provides information, it references the specific document and passage it was drawn from, ensuring transparency and reliability. Though it is always good to verify when using generative AI tools.

With the Deep Dive Audio feature, NotebookLM can also create podcasts based on reading materials that summarize dense material. This feature makes academic content more accessible and adaptable to different learning preferences and can be used, with faculty supervision, to enhance comprehension and engagement with the materials. It can also produce these podcasts in multiple languages, increasing access to new disciplines for non-native English speakers.

 

How to get started with NotebookLM?

1. Sign in to NotebookLM using your Google account. On the main page, click on “Try NotebookLM,” which will direct you to the page where you can create a new notebook.

2. Create a new notebook, where you upload different documents (lecture materials, research papers, textbooks, etc.) and interact with them through a chatbot.

3. After uploading the sources, you can interact with their documents in various ways, such as analyzing key information, generating discussion questions, summarizing content, and generating a “Deep Dive” podcast conversation.

Using NotebookLM as a Pedagogical Tool

  • Upload textbooks, research articles, or lecture notes, and use NotebookLM to generate structured summaries that highlight key concepts and main ideas.

  • NotebookLM can generate reading guides with annotations and key takeaways to help students navigate complex material. It can also create reading comprehension and discussion questions to encourage deeper engagement with the text.

    • Faculty can use it to highlight key themes, clarify difficult concepts, and connect readings to broader course topics. 

    • However, while NotebookLM has a relatively low hallucination rate, it’s important to manually review the summaries and questions it generates to ensure accuracy and relevance.

  • Based on uploaded documents, NotebookLM can suggest questions tailored to specific learning objectives and create quizzes, case studies, or practical exercises.

  • NotebookLM makes it easier to manage course materials and streamline teaching by:

    • Finding Key Passages Quickly: With uploaded textbooks, research papers, and lecture notes, faculty can quickly search for specific quotes, key arguments, or definitions when preparing for class or responding to student questions.
    • Keeping Course Materials Organized: In NotebookLM, faculty can organize readings by topic, week, or theme, making it easier to access relevant materials when planning lectures or discussions.
    • Providing Context for Student Questions: When students ask about a concept, faculty can retrieve relevant excerpts from course materials to support clear, accurate explanations.
    • Supporting Office Hours and Student Feedback: Faculty can store student questions and past discussions in a notebook, allowing for more personalized follow-ups and helping students connect ideas over time.

Guidelines for Using NotebookLM in Research and Teaching

While NotebookLM can be a valuable tool for organizing and engaging with course materials, it is important to approach its use with careful consideration.

  • Organization and Citation Management: It is important to verify that the citations generated align with the required citation style, as automated tools may occasionally require adjustments.
  • Ensuring Accuracy and Reliability: While NotebookLM has a relatively low hallucination rate, AI-generated content should always be reviewed for accuracy. Faculty are encouraged to cross-check summaries and citations against original sources before integrating them into their work.
  • Guiding Students in Responsible AI Use: As AI becomes more accessible, students may rely on it in ways that do not align with the learning goals. Faculty can help students understand how to engage with AI tools thoughtfully, emphasizing their role in supporting comprehension rather than replacing independent analysis.

By using NotebookLM intentionally and within academic guidelines, you can explore its potential benefits while maintaining high standards of scholarly integrity.

Enhancing Accessibility and Engagement with Deep Dive Audio Podcast Feature

Traditional academic readings may at times be challenging for neurodiverse learners and students from diverse linguistic and academic backgrounds (for instance, students who have studied in languages other than English prior to coming to NYUAD). Using the Deep Dive Audio feature can enhance the accessibility of course readings and increase student engagement. Faculty can use it as a supplement for traditional reading assignments by generating audio versions of key readings and turning them into personalized podcasts.

Generating AI Podcasts with NotebookLM

    1. Upload readings or course materials to NotebookLM.
    2. The AI converts the text into a structured, spoken format, ensuring a natural, engaging listening experience.
    3. You can prompt the software to customize the podcast by focusing on specific themes or key concepts to align the audio content with the course objectives. 
    4. Review the AI-generated transcript to verify accuracy and ensure no AI hallucinations before sharing it with students, and ensuring that all students have access to the same reliable version of the material while still allowing space for thoughtful interpretation and discussion.
    5. Specify to students how they should engage with the AI-generated material and what might be the advantages or drawbacks of incorporating it in their learning. If you wish students to still engage with the original text, despite having access to the podcast, explain why you are asking them to do that. 

    Sample Use

    "Create a podcast on the three readings for Mind, Matter, and the Machine, aligning with the syllabus and course learning outcomes. Ensure students grasp key ideas and can answer:

    (1) What is the basic result of the neuroscientific experiment?

    (2) How might one resist the idea that the experiment challenges free will?-"

    In a few minutes, NotebookLM generates a short podcast, which you can listen to in order to fact-check for any hallucinations. Once you are sure that it fits the requirements, you can upload it as an audio file on Brightspace for students to listen to.

    You can also share a link to the project directly to students:

  • NotebookLM’s Interactive Mode offers a way for students to engage with course materials by allowing them to join live conversations between the podcast hosts. This feature enables students to become active participants, asking questions and discussing key topics as they arise during the podcast.

    It’s particularly useful after reading assigned texts, helping students clarify confusing points or deepen their understanding through real-time discussions. Even while listening, learners can pause and contribute questions that reflect their thought processes, making the experience dynamic and responsive. This approach introduces a new, interactive form of learning — like having a personal tutor who guides students through complex material, simulating a conversation with the professor. The feature transforms passive listening into an engaging learning session, offering a unique opportunity for students to explore course content collaboratively.

  • NotebookLM’s Deep Dive Audio feature promotes inclusive teaching by supporting diverse learning preferences and processing styles. Research shows that students engage with instructional materials in multiple ways — some retain information more effectively through reading, while others benefit from audio formats that allow for multitasking or accommodate different cognitive needs (CAST, 2018; Fleming & Mills, 1992).

    By offering both reading and audio options, NotebookLM enables learners to engage with content in the format that best supports their individual needs. This dual-modality approach aligns with Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles, which emphasize providing multiple means of representation to enhance accessibility and comprehension (CAST, 2018).

    Supporting Neurodivergent Learners

    The Deep Dive Audio feature is particularly beneficial for neurodivergent students. Students with ADHD, dyslexia, or other cognitive processing differences may find large blocks of text overwhelming or difficult to process. Research suggests that audio-based learning can reduce cognitive load and increase comprehension for these learners (Guerin et al., 2021; Schwanke et al., 2020).

    By offering course content in audio form, NotebookLM helps reduce the strain associated with traditional reading, making learning more approachable and less taxing for students who benefit from auditory processing or need to move at their own pace.

    Supporting Flexible Learning

    Audio delivery also meets the needs of students navigating complex schedules. Students can listen to Deep Dive summaries while commuting, exercising, or completing routine tasks — helping them stay connected to course content even when sitting down to read may not be feasible. Flexible access to materials in varied formats has been shown to increase student engagement and learning outcomes (Mayer, 2009).

  • A common concern among faculty is that tools like NotebookLM’s Deep Dive Audio feature may stop students from developing critical reading skills. Reading carefully and critically is undeniably a valuable skill that educators want their students to acquire. Therefore, it’s crucial to remember that NotebookLM  should be used to complement, not replace, reading. 

    A Supplement, Not a Substitute: Remind students and teach them that the audio feature serves as a supplementary tool. Students can listen to the podcast to get an initial overview, helping them grasp the main ideas before diving into the text. But reading the text themselves is crucial to student learning. Alternatively, they can read first and then listen to reinforce their understanding, or even skim the reading and use the audio to fill in comprehension gaps.

    Supporting Deeper Engagement: Audio learning can act as a primer that encourages more engaged reading. By familiarizing themselves with key concepts through listening, students may approach readings with a clearer sense of purpose, focusing on analysis rather than initial comprehension.

    Interactive Discussions Reinforce Critical Thinking: After either reading or listening, students can participate in the Interactive Mode discussions. This dynamic engagement ensures that the material is not passively consumed but actively processed, discussed, and questioned, thereby fostering critical thinking skills.

    Changing Nature of Reading in the Age of AI: While traditional reading skills remain important, the ways in which we engage with content are evolving. The ability to synthesize information from various formats — including audio — is becoming essential. Far from diminishing reading skills, tools like NotebookLM equip students with adaptable strategies for engaging with complex material.
