AI Basics: What You Need to Know Before Choosing an AI Tool

While it may be common to associate AI with complex technology such as robotics and autonomous vehicles, AI is also used in many of the things we interact with on a daily basis. From mobile devices to online services, AI powers facial recognition, self-driving systems, predictive text messaging, voice assistants, email filters, navigation apps, and web-based search engines.

Types of AI

Not all AI works the same way. Understanding the differences helps you know what each type can and cannot do:

Traditional AI (Narrow AI)

  • Designed to perform specific tasks like spam filtering, recommendation algorithms, or voice recognition
  • Follows predefined rules and patterns
  • Examples: Netflix recommendations, Siri/Alexa, autocorrect, spam filters
  • Cannot perform tasks outside its specific programming

Machine Learning (ML)

  • AI systems that learn patterns from data without being explicitly programmed for every scenario
  • Improves performance over time with more data
  • Examples: fraud detection, image recognition, predictive analytics
  • Subset of AI focused on pattern recognition and prediction

Generative AI (GenAI)

  • Creates new content (text, images, audio, video, code) based on patterns learned from training data
  • Responds to user prompts to generate human-like outputs
  • Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, DALL-E, Midjourney, GitHub Copilot
  • Works through statistical prediction, not comprehension or fact-checking

 

Ithaka S+R partnered with 19 US and Canadian institutions to track the rapidly evolving GenAI product marketplace for higher education, creating a publicly available Product Tracker with 100+ tools marketed specifically for teaching, learning, and research: GenAI Product Tracker

Capabilities & Limitations of GenAI

Within appropriate guidelines, GenAI tools can be useful for many learning and research applications, but it is important to know the difference between what it can and cannot do. 

Capabilities:

  • Brainstorming ideas and exploring topics
  • Explaining complex concepts in accessible language
  • Drafting and revising writing
  • Generating summaries of long documents
  • Creating study materials and practice questions
  • Translating languages
  • Writing and debugging code
  • Creating images, presentations, or other visual content

Limitations:

  • Cannot think, reason, or understand: GenAI predicts the next word based on patterns, it doesn't comprehend meaning
  • Cannot replace critical thinking: It generates confident, plausible-sounding text to prioritize coherence, not truth or accuracy
  • Cannot provide accurate information without verification: It can frequently fabricate sources (hallucinate), misrepresent real information, incorrectly summarize information
  • Cannot make ethical judgments: It has no moral framework or accountability
  • Cannot feel emotion or empathy: It simulates tone but has no lived experience
  • Cannot access real-time information: Most models have knowledge cutoff dates and may not browse the internet (unless specifically designed or instructed to)
  • Cannot guarantee originality: Outputs are an average response generated from the entire spectrum of training data, and may reflect or reproduce training data in problematic ways
