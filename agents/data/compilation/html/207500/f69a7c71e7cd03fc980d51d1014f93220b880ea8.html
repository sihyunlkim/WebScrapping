Welcome to AI in the Libraries

As Artificial Intelligence (AI) becomes increasingly common, it is important to know the basic ins and outs of AI as an evolving technology that is here to stay. This guide is designed to enhance AI literacy within the OU community by offering resources to explore different types of AI, navigate foundational tools and research applications, understand data safety and privacy, and examine the ethical implications of AI use.

AI Terms and Glossary

  • Artificial Intelligence: Artificial intelligence (AI) is a system or machine that simulates human intelligence through programming to mimic the behaviors and thought processes of human beings. Modern AI is focused on creating machines that are capable of learning, making decisions, and adapting to tasks through pattern recognition and perception of natural language.
  • Machine Learning: Machine learning (ML) is a subset of AI that enables computers to learn from structured data and improve their performance over time. By identifying patterns in datasets, ML systems can make predictions, classify information, and optimize decision-making without being explicitly programmed for each specific task.
    • A Neural Network (NN) is a subset of ML that is made by connecting several layers (that transform data) and "nodes" (that transfer information) to improve speed and accuracy over time.
    • Deep learning is subset of ML that uses multi-layered NNs to transform complex data and automate tasks with minimal human interaction.
  • Natural Language Processing: Natural language processing (NLP) is a subset of AI that is used to understand and interpret interactions between machines and human language. NLP includes speech and text processing, natural language generation, and text classification.
  • Generative AI: Generative AI (GenAI) broadly encompasses AI models that are designed to respond to user requests, or "prompts," to generate new content such as text, images, and videos. GenAI models are built off deep learning architectures, and predict responses based on the training data.
  • Large Language Model: A large language model (LLM) is a program trained on enormous collections of text and code. LLMs work by analyzing millions of examples to identify statistical patterns in how words typically follow one another. LLMs operate through statistical prediction rather than genuine comprehension. When you prompt an LLM, it generates responses by calculating which word is most likely to come next based on these learned patterns, then repeating this process word by word. This pattern-matching technology powers the chatbots and tools commonly referred to as generative pretrained transformers (GPTs).
  • Prompt Engineering: A method for giving large language models specific instructions or examples to get the best possible output. How you ask something in a GenAI chat is just as important as what you're asking, since LLMs are trained on datasets that are reflective of the society and language behind them. The practice of writing good prompts with detailed instructions, context, and conversational feedback helps reduce the chance for models to make things up, or "hallucinate."

    From Learn Prompting: This course is tailored to non-technical readers, who may not have even heard of AI, making it the perfect starting point if you are new to Generative AI and Prompt Engineering.
  • The CLEAR path: A framework for enhancing information literacy through prompt engineering
    This article introduces the CLEAR Framework for Prompt Engineering, designed to optimize interactions with AI language models like ChatGPT. The framework encompasses five core principles—Concise, Logical, Explicit, Adaptive, and Reflective—that facilitate more effective AI-generated content evaluation and creation.

Chain-of-Thought prompting asks AI to show its reasoning process step-by-step, similar to "showing your work" in math class. This technique helps prevent incorrect output, and can be used to understand how to arrive at an answer, making it valuable for breaking down complex concepts and mitigating cognitive offload.

Learning Example: Studying

I'm studying the French Revolution for my history exam. Help me understand 
the causes by thinking through this step by step:

1. What were the economic conditions in France before 1789?
2. How did these conditions affect different social classes?
3. What were the political factors?
4. How did these factors interact to create revolutionary conditions?

Walk me through each step with evidence.

 

Research Example: Lit Review

I'm researching the impact of social media on teenage mental health. 
Think step by step to help me:

1. What are the main theories connecting social media use and mental health?
2. What does recent empirical research show (last 5 years)?
3. What are the gaps or contradictions in current research?
4. Based on this analysis, what's a viable research question I could pursue?

One-shot prompting provides a single example to demonstrate what you want, while few-shot prompting provides 2-5 examples to teach AI your specific standards, format, or style. Think of this as showing AI a model before asking it to replicate the pattern. This can be useful when you need to match academic conventions, citation styles, or specific assignment requirements.

One-Shot Learning Example: Quick Format

Convert this source to APA 7th edition format:

Example:
Book by Smith, J. (2020), titled "Digital Literacy" published by Oxford Press
→ Smith, J. (2020). Digital literacy. Oxford University Press.

Now format this source:
Book by Garcia, M. (2023), titled "AI Ethics" published by MIT Press

 

Few-Shot Learning Example: Citation Practice

Convert these sources to APA 7th edition format:

Example 1:
Book by Smith, published 2020, titled "Digital Literacy" by Oxford Press
→ Smith, J. (2020). Digital literacy. Oxford University Press.

Example 2:
Journal article by Jones and Lee, 2023, "AI in Education" in Journal of 
Learning Technology, volume 15, pages 234-250
→ Jones, M., & Lee, S. (2023). AI in education. Journal of Learning 
Technology, 15, 234-250.

Example 3:
Website by Brown, 2024, "Understanding Neural Networks" on Tech Insights
→ Brown, R. (2024). Understanding neural networks. Tech Insights. 
https://www.techinsights.com/neural-networks

Now format this source:
Online article by Garcia, Rodriguez, and Kim published January 2024...

Write an annotated bibliography entry following this model:

Example:
Chen, L. (2023). Machine learning in healthcare diagnosis. Medical AI Review, 
12(3), 145-167.

This peer-reviewed article examines machine learning algorithms used in 
diagnostic imaging. Chen's meta-analysis of 50 studies demonstrates 92%

Few-Shot Research Example: Coding Qualitative Data

I'm coding interview transcripts about student experiences with AI tools. 
Here are examples:

Quote: "I felt like I was cheating even though it was allowed"
→ Code: Ethical ambiguity, Imposter feelings

Quote: "It helped me understand the concept better than the textbook"
→ Code: Learning enhancement, Pedagogical value

Quote: "I spent more time fixing AI errors than if I'd written it myself"
→ Code: Efficiency paradox, Time management

Now code this quote: "My professor never explained if we could use it or not"

 

Role-based prompting assigns AI a specific persona, expertise level, or perspective (e.g., "You are a biology professor" or "Act as a peer reviewer"). This technique helps you get explanations at the appropriate level of complexity and access different viewpoints. It can be useful for bridging knowledge gaps or simulating expert feedback.

Learning Example: Understanding Complex Topics

You are a biology professor explaining to first-year students.

Explain CRISPR gene editing using an analogy that relates to editing 
a document. Keep it under 150 words and avoid jargon.

 or, 

You are a teaching assistant helping a student who understands basic algebra 
but struggles with calculus concepts.

Explain the concept of derivatives without using formal mathematical notation. 
Use real-world examples like speed and distance.

 

Research Example: Peer Review Simulation

You are an experienced peer reviewer for an undergraduate research journal 
in sociology. 

Review this abstract for: (1) clarity of research question, (2) appropriate 
methodology, (3) contribution to the field. Provide constructive feedback 
a student could act on.

[Abstract text]

 

Research Example: Multiple Perspectives  

You are a historian specializing in oral history methods.

Evaluate my interview protocol for a project on community responses to 
gentrification. Consider: question bias, cultural sensitivity, and 
historical context.

[Abstract text]

ReAct prompting combines thinking (reasoning) with doing (acting), creating a cycle of Thought → Action → Observation that mirrors the real research process. You explicitly plan what information you need, gather it, analyze what you found, and decide on next steps. This technique works for complex research tasks that require verification, multiple sources, or iterative inquiry.

Research Example - Source Verification:

Question: Is this claim from my source accurate: "Over 80% of academic 
libraries now offer AI literacy instruction"?

Thought 1: I need to verify this statistic with recent sources.
Action 1: Search for "academic library AI literacy instruction statistics 2024"
Observation 1: [Results found]

Thought 2: The original claim seems inflated. I should look for actual surveys.
Action 2: Search for "ACRL library AI instruction survey"
Observation 2: [Findings]

Thought 3: I need to check if this is US-only or international data.
Action 3: Review the methodology of the survey I found
Observation 3: [Context clarified]

Conclusion: Revise my paper to reflect accurate statistics and cite the 
primary source with appropriate context about survey scope.

 

Research Example: Gap Analysis

My research question: How do community college students use AI differently 
than university students?

Thought 1: I should check what existing research says about community college 
students and AI.
Action 1: Search academic databases for "community college AND artificial 
intelligence AND students"
Observation 1: Only 12 peer-reviewed articles found, mostly about institutional 
implementation.

Thought 2: That's surprisingly few. Let me check the broader literature on 
university students.
Action 2: Search "undergraduate AND AI tool use AND learning"
Observation 2: Over 200 articles, mostly focused on R1 institutions.

Thought 3: I should verify whether community colleges are included in the 
broader undergraduate studies.
Action 3: Review samples and populations in the top 20 university studies
Observation 3: Only 3 mention community colleges; all others are 4-year institutions

Analysis: This gap confirms my research could make an original contribution. 
Community college student perspectives are significantly underrepresented 
in current literature.

 

Research Example: Methodology 

Task: Design a survey to measure student attitudes toward AI writing tools

Thought 1: I need to understand what dimensions other researchers have measured
Action 1: Search for "survey instrument AI writing tools attitudes"
Observation 1: Found 3 validated instruments measuring: usefulness, ease of use, 
ethical concerns, learning impact

Thought 2: Are these instruments tested with my population (undergraduates)?
Action 2: Check the samples in each study
Observation 2: Two used graduate students, one used faculty. None tested 
with undergrads.

Thought 3: I should adapt existing validated items rather than create new ones
Action 3: Review the item wording and licensing for adaptation
Observation 3: Two instruments allow educational use with attribution

Decision: Adapt items from Studies A and B, pilot test with 20 students, 
refine based on cognitive interviews about item clarity.

Structured output prompting specifies the exact format you want AI to use (tables, JSON, specific headings, numbered lists). Instead of getting narrative paragraphs, you get information that is organized in a way that is easier to compare, analyze, or integrate into your research. This can be valuable for systematic reviews, content analysis, or any research requiring data extraction across multiple sources.

Formatting Types

Labeled Fields Format: 

  • Uses bold labels followed by colons to create clear field names
  • Brackets [ ] indicate where AI should insert information
  • Provides options in brackets (e.g., [qualitative/quantitative/mixed methods]) to constrain responses
  • Creates a consistent key-value structure that's easy to scan
  • Works well for extracting 5-10 pieces of information from a single source
**Research Design:** [qualitative/quantitative/mixed methods]
**Sample Size:** [number and population]
**Key Finding:** [one sentence]

Best for: Single article summaries, annotated bibliographies, literature review notes
Output to: Word docs, Google Docs, note-taking apps
Use this format if you want text that is human-readable and easy to scan

 

Table/Matrix: 

  • Uses Markdown table syntax with pipes | and dashes -
  • Creates columns for different types of information
  • Provides rows for systematic comparison across items
  • Headers define what goes in each column
  • Empty cells show AI exactly where to insert data
| Element | Present (Y/N) | Specific Language | Location |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|----------|
| AI policy | | | |
| Citations required | | | |

Best for: Comparing multiple sources, content analysis, systematic reviews
Output to: Excel, Google Sheets, data analysis software
Use this format if you want to do side-by-side comparisons, pattern identification, or counting/statistics

 

Hierarchical: 

  • Uses Markdown heading levels (## for main sections, ### for subsections)
  • Creates a hierarchical structure with parent-child relationships
  • Uses bullet points for multiple items under each category
  • Includes metadata like line numbers in parentheses
  • Organizes information by conceptual groupings (themes)

## Participant ID: [number]

### Theme 1: Benefits
- Quote 1: "[exact text]" (line 45)
- Quote 2: "[exact text]" (line 67)

### Theme 2: Concerns
- Quote 1: "[exact text]" (line 102)

Best for: Qualitative data analysis, organizing themes with examples, interview coding
Output to: NVivo, Atlas.ti, qualitative analysis software, organized notes
Use this format if you want to preserve relationships between themes and data, maintain traceability, and mirror research paper structures

Prompt chaining breaks one large, complex task into a sequence of smaller, connected prompts where each output becomes the input for the next step. Think of it as a research pipeline where instead of asking AI to "write a literature review," you chain together prompts for identifying themes, analyzing each theme, synthesizing findings, and identifying any gaps. This produces higher-quality results by allowing you to guide and refine the process at each stage.

Research Example: Developing a Research Question

PROMPT 1: "List 5 current controversies in higher education related to AI 
that lack sufficient research"
OUTPUT 1: [List of controversies]

PROMPT 2: "Take controversy #3 from this list: [paste]. Identify which 
stakeholder groups are most affected and what questions they would ask."
OUTPUT 2: [Stakeholder analysis]

PROMPT 3: "Based on these stakeholders: [paste], formulate 3 specific, 
researchable questions that could be addressed in an undergraduate thesis 
(15-20 pages)."
OUTPUT 3: [Research questions]

PROMPT 4: "Evaluate research question #2: [paste] for: feasibility (available 
sources), scope (appropriate for thesis), originality (fills a gap). Suggest 
refinements."
OUTPUT 4: [Final refined question]

 

Research Example: Lit Review 

 

PROMPT 1: "Review these 10 abstracts and identify recurring themes. List 
themes with the number of articles addressing each."
OUTPUT 1: [Theme list with counts]

PROMPT 2: "For Theme 1 '[paste theme]' found in [X] articles, what are the 
main arguments or findings? Organize by agreement vs. disagreement."
OUTPUT 2: [Analysis of Theme 1]

PROMPT 3: "Repeat for Theme 2: [paste theme]"
OUTPUT 3: [Analysis of Theme 2]

PROMPT 4: "Based on themes 1-3 [paste summaries], what gaps, contradictions, 
or unexplored questions emerge? Prioritize by research potential."
OUTPUT 4: [Gap analysis]

PROMPT 5: "Draft a 300-word literature review synthesis covering [paste themes], 
highlighting the primary gap: [paste main gap]. Use academic tone."
OUTPUT 5: [Draft synthesis]

 

Research Example: Survey Development

PROMPT 1: "Based on my research question [paste], what constructs should I 
measure? Define each construct."
OUTPUT 1: [Construct definitions]

PROMPT 2: "For construct 1 '[paste]', generate 5 potential survey items 
using a 5-point Likert scale. Follow best practices for item clarity."
OUTPUT 2: [Items for construct 1]

PROMPT 3: "Review these items [paste]: check for: double-barreled questions, 
leading language, ambiguity, appropriate response options."
OUTPUT 3: [Item critique]

PROMPT 4: "Revise the flagged items based on this feedback [paste]. Provide 
improved versions."
OUTPUT 4: [Revised items]

PROMPT 5: "Organize all final items [paste] into a logical survey flow with 
section headings and instructions."
OUTPUT 5: [Complete survey draft]

 

Constraint specification prompting explicitly defines what must be included, what must be avoided, and what boundaries apply (word count, format, sources, audience, etc.). This technique makes sure that AI output meets your specific academic requirements and assignment criteria. It can be useful for assignments with detailed rubrics or when working within strict disciplinary conventions.

Learning Example: Writing to Requirements 

Write a thesis statement for my paper on AI bias in hiring algorithms.

REQUIREMENTS:
- Must be exactly one sentence
- Must include: main argument + 3 supporting reasons
- Must be debatable (not just factual)
- Must use academic tone
- Cannot exceed 35 words

CONTEXT: This is for a 10-page undergraduate research paper in 
organizational psychology.

 

Research Example: Lit Search 

Help me develop search terms for my research on student perceptions of 
AI-generated feedback.

MUST INCLUDE:
- Synonym variations (at least 3 for each main concept)
- Both US and UK terminology differences
- Appropriate Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT)
- Truncation symbols where appropriate

MUST EXCLUDE:
- K-12 contexts (focus only on higher ed)
- Corporate training contexts
- Pre-2020 terminology that's now outdated
- General "feedback" without AI specification

DELIVERABLE: Provide 3 complete search strings ready to use in academic 
databases, formatted as:
(concept 1 terms) AND (concept 2 terms) AND (concept 3 terms) NOT (exclusions)

 

Tree of Thoughts prompting explores multiple solution paths simultaneously, evaluating each approach before committing to one. Unlike linear prompting that follows a single path, ToT creates branches (like a decision tree) where you can compare different methodologies, theoretical frameworks, or analytical strategies. This technique can help support metacognitive awareness by making your decision-making process explicit and helping you choose the strongest approach with clear justification.

Research Example: Methodology 

I'm studying how undergraduate writing changes when AI tools are available.
Evaluate possible research approaches:

APPROACH 1: Quantitative comparison
- Design: Compare writing samples with/without AI (controlled experiment)
- Measure: Grammar scores, syntactic complexity, originality metrics
- Strengths: Measurable data, statistical analysis possible
- Weaknesses: Misses the "why," hard to control all variables, reductive
- Feasibility: MEDIUM - need IRB approval, recruiting challenges
- Viability: 6/10

APPROACH 2: Qualitative interviews  
- Design: In-depth interviews with 15-20 students about their writing process
- Analyze: Thematic analysis of decision-making with AI
- Strengths: Rich data, captures nuance and reasoning, flexibility
- Weaknesses: Not generalizable, time-intensive transcription
- Feasibility: HIGH - easier IRB, manageable in one semester
- Viability: 8/10

APPROACH 3: Mixed methods
- Design: Survey (n=100) + follow-up interviews (n=15)
- Analyze: Descriptive stats + thematic analysis
- Strengths: Most comprehensive, combines breadth and depth
- Weaknesses: Time-intensive, requires both quant and qual expertise
- Feasibility: LOW - too ambitious for thesis timeline
- Viability: 4/10

APPROACH 4: Case study
- Design: Follow 3-5 students through entire writing process with AI
- Track: Process logs, drafts, think-aloud protocols
- Strengths: Deep understanding of actual practice, contextual richness
- Weaknesses: Very small sample, intensive time commitment from participants
- Feasibility: MEDIUM - need dedicated participants
- Viability: 7/10

DECISION: Approach 2 (qualitative interviews) provides the best balance 
of rigor, feasibility, and alignment with my research interest in understanding 
student reasoning. The "why" questions are more important than quantification 
for this exploratory topic. Approach 4 could be future research after 
establishing themes.

 

Research Example: Theoretical Framework

My research examines why students choose to use or avoid AI writing tools.
Which theoretical lens is most appropriate?

FRAMEWORK 1: Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
- Focus: Perceived usefulness + ease of use → intention to use
- Application: Survey measuring these constructs
- Strengths: Well-validated, parsimonious, testable
- Limitations: Doesn't capture ethical concerns or social pressure
- Fit with research question: 7/10

FRAMEWORK 2: Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
- Focus: Autonomy, competence, relatedness as motivators
- Application: Analyze how AI affects these psychological needs
- Strengths: Explains intrinsic motivation, fits educational context
- Limitations: Complex to operationalize, needs qualitative approach
- Fit with research question: 8/10

FRAMEWORK 3: Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)
- Focus: Attitudes + norms + perceived control → behavior
- Application: Examines social influence and self-efficacy alongside attitudes
- Strengths: Captures peer/instructor influence, comprehensive
- Limitations: Requires measuring multiple constructs, overlapping concepts
- Fit with research question: 9/10

FRAMEWORK 4: Academic Identity Theory
- Focus: How tool use relates to identity as student/writer
- Application: Qualitative exploration of identity threat/enhancement
- Strengths: Addresses deeper meaning-making, novel application
- Limitations: Less structured, harder to measure, limited precedent
- Fit with research question: 6/10

DECISION: Theory of Planned Behavior (Framework 3) best captures the 
multi-faceted nature of the decision, including social dynamics that other 
models miss. The construct overlap is manageable with careful item development, 
and extensive precedent exists for educational technology contexts.

 

Research Example: Analytical 

I have 20 interviews about faculty experiences implementing AI policies.
What analytical approach should I use?

APPROACH A: Inductive thematic analysis
- Process: Code without predetermined themes, let patterns emerge
- Strengths: Discovers unexpected findings, honors participant voice
- Time required: 40-50 hours
- Outcome type: 5-7 data-driven themes
- Best if: Exploratory research, little existing theory

APPROACH B: Deductive framework analysis  
- Process: Use existing literature categories (e.g., barriers, facilitators, outcomes)
- Strengths: Directly answers specific questions, easier to communicate
- Time required: 25-30 hours
- Outcome type: Analysis organized by predetermined framework
- Best if: Testing or extending existing models

APPROACH C: Hybrid thematic + narrative
- Process: Identify themes but preserve individual stories
- Strengths: Shows patterns AND illustrates diversity of experience
- Time required: 50-60 hours
- Outcome type: Themes with case vignettes
- Best if: Complexity and individual context matter

APPROACH D: Grounded theory
- Process: Constant comparison, theoretical sampling, develop new theory
- Strengths: Generates novel theoretical insights
- Time required: 80-100 hours
- Outcome type: Theoretical model
- Best if: Sufficient time, theory-building goal

DECISION: Approach A (inductive thematic analysis) is most appropriate. 
This is an emerging area where faculty experiences are not yet well-documented, 
so an exploratory approach will yield the most valuable insights. Time 
requirement is manageable for a thesis timeline. Can incorporate elements 
of narrative (Approach C) in the findings section by using extended quotes 
as illustrations.

 

Meta Prompting as system-level Instructions provide overarching instructions that govern how AI behaves across all interactions, much like setting the "rules of engagement" behind the scenes. Examples of system-level meta prompts:

  • Custom instructions in ChatGPT (the "About me" and "How should ChatGPT respond" fields)
  • System prompts in Claude Projects
  • Custom GPT instructions that define personality, constraints, and approach
  • Persona definitions that persist across conversations

Cross-Platform Tip: You can also use meta-prompting to generate custom instructions or system prompts for any AI platform. Ask one AI tool to create optimized instructions for itself or for other platforms (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, etc.), taking into account each platform's specific capabilities, limitations, and interface features such as context windows. This is especially useful when setting up custom GPTs, Claude Projects, or configuring system prompts for different model versions (e.g. ChatGPT-4o vs ChatGPT-5, Claude Haiku vs Claude Sonnet).

Example: Custom Instructions

I need to set up custom instructions in ChatGPT for academic research support.

Create system-level instructions that:
- Define my role as an undergraduate psychology researcher
- Establish citation standards (APA 7th edition)
- Set academic integrity boundaries
- Optimize for ChatGPT's strengths and limitations

Provide two sections:
1. "What would you like ChatGPT to know about you?"
2. "How would you like ChatGPT to respond?"

Keep each section under 1500 characters (ChatGPT's limit).

 

Example: Cross-Platform Instructions

I use both ChatGPT and Claude for research. Create equivalent system prompts 
for each platform that establish the same academic standards but account for:

ChatGPT considerations:
- Browsing capability for current information
- Plugin/tool integration
- Conversation memory features

Claude considerations:
- Longer context window (better for document analysis)
- More detailed reasoning explanations
- Project knowledge bases

Provide optimized instructions for each platform.

 

Example: Model-Specific Instructions

I'm setting up a custom GPT using GPT-4. What instructions should I include to:
- Maximize its reasoning capabilities
- Compensate for known limitations (knowledge cutoff, etc.)
- Leverage its multimodal features
- Ensure consistent academic tone
- Build in verification prompts

Draft system instructions optimized specifically for GPT-4's architecture.

 

Research Example: Research Assistant  

In the custom instructions section of your AI tool, enter:

CONTEXT ABOUT YOU:
I'm an undergraduate researcher in psychology studying [topic].
I need help with literature reviews and methodology.
I have access to my university library databases.

HOW AI SHOULD RESPOND:
- Always distinguish between your suggestions and established methods
- Provide specific database search strategies
- Ask about my timeline and resources before suggesting approaches
- Point me to primary sources, not summaries
- Remind me to verify claims in peer-reviewed literature
- Use academic language but explain jargon

 

Example: Library Support Context

 SYSTEM INSTRUCTION:
You are a reference librarian assistant. In all responses:
- Prioritize teaching research skills over giving direct answers
- Recommend specific databases and library resources
- Frame responses as collaborative inquiry ("Let's explore...")
- Link to relevant LibGuides when applicable
- Defer to human librarians for policy questions

 

Example: Student Writing Context

 SYSTEM INSTRUCTION:
You are a writing tutor, not a writing service. Always:
- Ask questions that prompt student thinking
- Explain WHY, not just WHAT to change
- Refuse to write complete assignments
- Focus on teaching transferable skills
- Use the Socratic method
- Celebrate student agency and decision-making

 or, 

CUSTOM INSTRUCTION:
When I ask about citations:
- Always specify APA 7th edition unless I request otherwise
- Show the format, then explain the pattern
- Ask if I need in-text citation, reference list, or both
- Remind me to verify against the actual source
- Note common errors for that source type

 

Example: Academic Context

 SYSTEM INSTRUCTION:
You are assisting a graduate student in education research. Always:
- Cite sources when making factual claims
- Ask clarifying questions before making assumptions
- Provide APA 7th edition citations
- Flag when you're uncertain
- Suggest where to verify information
- Use academic tone but remain accessible

Meta-prompting uses AI to analyze, critique, and improve your prompts themselves. Instead of immediately asking AI to complete a task, you first ask it to evaluate your prompt for clarity, completeness, potential issues, and suggest improvements. This technique can help you develop stronger prompting skills over time and makes sure that your instructions will produce the results you actually need.

 

Learning Example: Assignment Prompts

I'm about to use this prompt for a class assignment. Evaluate it first:

MY PROMPT: "Help me write about the causes of World War I"

Your evaluation should assess:
1. Clarity - Is it clear what kind of help I want?
2. Specificity - Does it provide enough constraints?
3. Academic appropriateness - Does it respect learning goals?
4. Likely output quality - What will this prompt actually produce?

Then provide an improved version of my prompt.

 

Research Example: Research Prompts

Analyze this prompt I plan to use for literature review help:

MY PROMPT: "Summarize research on online learning effectiveness"

Evaluate for:
- Scope clarity (is "online learning" too broad?)
- Temporal boundaries (what time period?)
- Specificity of "effectiveness" (measured how?)
- Output expectations (format, length, depth?)
- Missing context (what's my research focus?)

Provide a refined version that will yield more useful results for academic research.

 

Research Example: Survey Question Improvement

 I want to use AI to help develop survey items. First, evaluate my planned prompt:

MY PROMPT: "Write 10 survey questions about student stress"

Meta-analysis needed:
- Does this specify the type of questions (open/closed, scale)?
- Does it indicate the target construct clearly enough?
- Does it mention validity or reliability considerations?
- Does it specify what population or context?
- What problems might this prompt create?

Suggest an improved prompt that incorporates best practices for survey 
instrument development.

 

Research Example: Iterative Prompt

ROUND 1: Review this prompt and identify weaknesses:
"Help me code my interview data about student experiences with AI"

[AI provides critique]

ROUND 2: Here's my revised prompt based on your feedback:
"Review this interview transcript [paste] and suggest codes related to 
student emotional responses to using AI tools. Use gerunds for code names."

Is this better? What's still missing?

[AI provides second-level critique]

ROUND 3: Final version incorporating all feedback:
[Your refined prompt]

Confirm this prompt will produce: [list specific outcomes you need]

 

Calibrated confidence prompting asks AI to indicate its level of certainty alongside its responses, helping you assess reliability and identify when additional verification is needed. This technique promotes healthy skepticism toward AI outputs and encourages users to think critically about when information can be trusted versus when it requires fact-checking and contextualization with authoritative sources.

Learning Example: Concept Understanding

Explain the concept of "algorithmic bias" for my AI ethics paper.

After your explanation, provide:
- Confidence in explanation accuracy: [High/Medium/Low]
- Confidence in completeness: [High/Medium/Low]
- Potential gaps: [what might be missing]
- Recommended verification: [what textbook/article to consult]

This helps me understand what I can use directly and what needs additional research.

 

Research Example: Source Claim Verification 

Question: What percentage of undergraduate students used generative AI for 
coursework in Fall 2024?

Provide your answer with:
1. The response
2. Confidence level (High/Medium/Low with percentage)
3. Reasoning for your confidence rating
4. What factors create uncertainty
5. What sources would increase confidence

Response format:
Answer: [your response]
Confidence: [HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW (X%)]
Reasoning: [why this confidence level]
Uncertainty factors: [what limits certainty]
Verification needed: [what sources to check]

 

Research Example: Lit Claim Assessment

Claim from my source: "Chain-of-thought prompting improves reasoning accuracy 
by 40% across all task types."

Evaluate this claim with:
- Accuracy assessment: [likely accurate/partially accurate/questionable/false]
- Confidence in assessment: [0-100%]
- Red flags identified: [list any concerns]
- What to verify: [specific checks needed]
- Suggested searches: [how to fact-check this]

Be explicit about what you can determine versus what requires checking primary sources.

 

Research Example: Methodological Advice

Question: Is a sample size of 50 adequate for thematic analysis of interview data?

Provide:
1. Direct answer
2. Confidence level with justification
3. Contextual factors affecting this answer
4. Disciplinary variations in standards
5. Where to find authoritative guidance

Structure:
ANSWER: [your response]
CONFIDENCE: [X%] because [reasoning]
CONTEXT MATTERS: [factors that change the answer]
DISCIPLINARY NOTES: [how standards vary]
AUTHORITATIVE SOURCES: [where to verify best practices]

Be explicit about the difference between general guidance and field-specific requirements.

 

Research Example: Statistical Interpretation

I found p = 0.049 in my analysis. The relationship is statistically significant, right?
