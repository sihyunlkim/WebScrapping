Teaching with Generative AI

  • Author By Barbara Knauff and Noah Lopez
  • Publication date October 10, 2025
  • (Updated on October 10, 2025 at 10:52 am)
  • Categories: Course Design & Preparation, Dartmouth Teaching & Learning, Teaching Practices
  • Categories: GenAI

With generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) tools readily available to both learners and instructors, many are concerned about the impacts on teaching and learning. What exactly are the capabilities and limitations of GenAI tools? What constitutes ethical or responsible use, if any? The growing prevalence of these tools is prompting us to ask fundamental questions about teaching and learning. What do we really want students to learn? How do we know that they’re learning it?

This resource offers a range of considerations and resources to help you begin to answer these questions and make the best decisions for your courses and assignments. Because this process is so context-dependent and unique to each teaching and learning situation, this resource is not intended to be definitive or prescriptive.

With that said, there is one recommendation we make to all instructors and courses, regardless of discipline or individual approach: 

Acknowledge the existence of GenAI tools to your students, and articulate your expectations of students in your course syllabus in as much detail as possible. Is it acceptable to use these tools in your course, or in specific assignments? In what ways? Is their use to be limited and, if so, how specifically (brainstorming, ideation, editing, debugging, etc.)? If you are incorporating these tools into your teaching and assignments, how should students document and cite their use of such technologies? 

The more explicit and transparent you are about the role of GenAI in your course, as well as your rationale for these decisions, the easier you make it for students to meet your expectations and have a successful learning experience.

GenAI at Dartmouth

All Dartmouth community members (instructors, staff, and students) have free access to a Dartmouth-hosted GenAI service: Dartmouth Chat. Dartmouth Chat is an AI chat app that supports various commercial large language models (LLMs) such as OpenAI’s GPT family, Google’s Gemini, and Anthropic’s Claude. The app also supports several open-source LLMs, including Meta’s Llama family. Access to all of these models is provided free of charge. We encourage you to take some time to explore the models available to you and get familiar with the Dartmouth Chat interface. 

In 2024, the Academic Honor Principle and its associated policies were updated to address the use of GenAI at Dartmouth, and Guidelines on Using GenAI in Coursework were recently established. While these policies aim to provide broad guidance across the institutional context, they operate at a high level only, leaving room for flexibility and necessitating interpretation within specific settings. This need for interpretation–for individual instructors and students within particular courses–is both a challenge and an opportunity. More specifically, the guidelines state that students may not use GenAI tools for submitted coursework unless expressly permitted by the instructor, and use of GenAI tools requires acknowledgement for academic integrity. Still, students may interpret this in a variety of ways in your specific context, so it is important to be explicit in your guidance and intentional in your planning as you are designing courses and assignments. 

If you have concerns about GenAI use outside of your expectations while a course is running, you can contact the Community Standards and Accountability Office for advice and potentially bring your concerns forward for review by the Committee on Standards. 

Integrating GenAI into Your Teaching

There are many ways to integrate GenAI into your teaching, should you choose to. The following are some concrete suggestions for your consideration:

  • Try it out: Explore the LLMs available at Dartmouth Chat. Prompt the tools with some of your actual assignment prompts (essay questions, homework instructions, test questions, etc.) to see what kind of output you get. Is the output wildly inadequate, could it pass as an acceptable submission, or does it produce something else? Going through this exercise gives you a point of departure for adapting your assignments. Also, remember: there is always an element of randomness in LLMs’ output and your testing will not produce identical results next time.
  • Emphasize process, not just product: Shift your assignment focus away from the final product and place a greater focus on the process. One way to do this is to scaffold your assignments to include steps such as proposals, first drafts, revisions, and reflections. For example, in a writing-focused course you might re-envision writing as a process of AI prompting, editing, and revising, building in reflection on whether and how this changes the writer’s thinking and learning processes.
  • Foreground metacognitive awareness: Include metacognitive components in your assignments whenever possible, to help students become aware of their own learning and be able to describe it. For instance, ask students to describe their process for completing a given assignment, what they learned both from the assignment itself and their reflection on it, and what lessons they are taking away for the future. “Explain your thinking” prompts can also be effective.
  • Spell out your learning goals: In a similar vein, make sure that students understand the learning goals behind their assignments and learning activities, so they can better evaluate whether using a GenAI tool would get in the way of accomplishing these learning goals, or whether it might assist them in their actual learning.
  • Rely on peer feedback: Consider including peer reviews, critiques, or responses in your assignment structure to engage students in a constructive dialogue about their work and their thinking. Peer review can be a very effective learning tool in a wide range of disciplines, but works best when carefully set up and deliberately modeled for students.
  • Analyze AI-generated content: Lean in to teaching with GenAI. For example, ask students to feed an assignment prompt into an LLM and critique the output. What are the strengths and weaknesses? Was it accurate and factual? Useful? Specific or generic? What, if anything, gives the output away as AI-generated? You might then ask: for the given task or problem, does it make sense to use GenAI tools? Why or why not? What becomes the role of us humans in such a scenario?
  • Create options: Offer dual versions of a given assignment: one where use of GenAI tools is permitted and one where it is not. Invite discussion around how achievement of your learning goals might be demonstrated even in the AI-assisted scenario, and require students who opt for this version to do so.

Ultimately, many of these suggestions come down to discerning exactly what it is you want students to learn and deliberately designing assessments and learning activities to support these learning goals.

As you craft your plan for engaging GenAI in your teaching, you also may want to consider examples of syllabus language and policies on using GenAI. What makes sense for your course and its unique learning objectives? Feel free to book a consultation with a member of our team if you’d like to discuss your ideas and possibilities. 

If you’re interested in learning more about how Dartmouth faculty are integrating GenAI into their teaching, check out the article about the GenAI Teaching Institute. 

Professional Development and Support

DCAL and LDI offer various opportunities for professional development and support for teaching with GenAI: 

  • Our GenAI Teaching Institute runs twice annually – in-person in December and online in the summer. The 2-day Institute is designed as an opportunity for a cohort of faculty to build their own GenAI literacy and design an assignment that effectively and responsibly integrates GenAI.
  • Much of DCAL and LDI programming beyond the institute addresses topics in teaching in the context of GenAI.
  • Our GenAI Teaching Grant Program pairs faculty grantees with a member of our team to consult on the implementation of GenAI in a course and gather student feedback. Grantees submit a description of their GenAI teaching intervention and reflect on the intervention alongside faculty peers. Read more about how the grant program features in our broader Teaching with GenAI Initiative.
  • Book a consultation with a member of our team to discuss how to best integrate or engage GenAI in your course(s)
  • Dartmouth Libraries regularly offers events and workshops on AI, and the Libraries’ Reproducible Research workshop series often features AI topics. 
  • If you’re looking to build your own skills and experience working with LLMs, check out our online worksheet on AI prompting and power user tips, featuring guided steps for exploring and experimenting with the LLMs available through Dartmouth Chat. Also, consider our further resources below!
