    Active Learning strategies 

    If you are teaching in an active learning classroom (with furniture specifically designed for active learning, such as in AAC 1048), these resources will be helpful to you. It is not required to have furniture to use these techniques; however, the furniture does make it easier.

    Steelcase created a helpful video about how to teach in an active learning classroom.

    Team-based learning is one excellent way to utilize an active learning space. Teams are developed at the start of the semester and students remain in those teams throughout the semester. Students will eventually assess each other's performance as a team member and assess their own performance as well.

    • University of Texas site on active/group learning
    • University of Texas video about group/team learning

    Group/team-based learning and flipped classroom are very similar. Essentially you prepare content for your students in advance. The students learn the material on their own (or with their classmates) outside of class and come prepared to apply their learning to problems you give them.

    Students should have a readiness test when they come into class so you know that they've prepared for the class session. That readiness test can be an individual test, or a combination of individual and team testing.

    Video about the flipped classroom

    • Notion4Teachers explores various active learning strategies with practical examples and implementation tips.
    • Eric Mazur, from Harvard University, demonstrates some active learning in his Physics class. He also shares information about utilizing peer instruction.
    • McGill University has a slightly different set up, but Rush might have something similar in the future. Check out what they're doing with their active learning classroom.

    Activities & Assignments

    What you have your students do should be the heart of your course. After all, they can learn the material from almost anywhere any more, and the real value in their education at Rush is what happens in the classroom, what they are doing with their learning, and their interactions with you and one another. For this section we're going to focus on what's happening in the class- traditional or virtual. Don't be afraid to try something new like project-based learning. Encourage students to apply their knowledge often, and have them create something to show for it!

    • Research: Team-based learning in health care education: Maintaining key design elements by Burgess & Mellis (2015)
    • High quality PBL (project-based learning) meets mental health
    • Faculty Focus: Could we be doing better with our assignments? 

    Another method of instruction includes problem-based learning. Students complete activities, assignments, and/or projects centered around a specific problem. The University of Delaware has a problem-based learning clearinghouse that's searchable. 

    • Research: Problem-based learning in allied health and medicine
    • What, how and why is problem-based learning in medical education?

    Formative Assessments

    Infusing regular opportunities for formative assessment is important in all courses. Sometimes it might be a quick, 3 question quiz for just a few points that students take after watching a video or reading instructor-developed text. It could be a short assessment in an online class that students can take as many times as they need to in order to pass it so they can move on to the next module. There's not one right way to assess students formatively; however, it is vitally important to use it often in your classes.

    • What is formative assessment and why use it? From Hunter College, SUNY
    • Formative Assessment Strategies by Jay Rasmussen from Bethel College

    Summative Assessments

    For many faculty members in the healthcare fields, grades are dependent upon a student passing a final assessment. Oftentimes a course has several of them, all high stakes, and mostly all of them traditional, true-false, multiple-choice exams. While that is tradition, it doesn't have to be the case for every assessment. Consider, instead, shorter, lower stakes formative assessments and infusing more authentic assessment within the course whenever feasible. 

    • Best practices in traditional test design
    • Alternatives to traditional testing from the University of California Berkeley
    • Multiple choice questions: Best practices from the University of South Florida
    • Research: Designing authentic assessment: Strategies for nurse educators 
    • Authentic assessment: Simulation-Based Education

  • Educational Research

    CTEI has provided our RUSH faculty with a curated list of reputable resources for educational research in both higher education and healthcare, encompassing journals, databases, and organizations that provide valuable insights and publications in these fields.

    1. ERIC (Education Resources Information Center)

      • A comprehensive, searchable online database offering access to a vast collection of education-related literature, including journal articles, research reports, and other resources.

    2. Higher Education Research Institute (HERI) – UCLA

      • An interdisciplinary center dedicated to research, evaluation, and policy studies in postsecondary education, providing valuable insights into various aspects of higher education.

    3. Journal of Open Educational Resources in Higher Education (JOERHE)

      • An open-access journal that publishes research articles on the use and impact of open educational resources in higher education, focusing on topics like open pedagogy and open data.

    4. OER Commons

      • A public digital library offering a wide range of open educational resources, including teaching materials and research publications, to support educators and researchers in higher education.

    5. ERIC and Other Clearinghouses

      • An overview of ERIC and other clearinghouses that provide access to a wealth of educational research and information, supporting educators, policymakers, and researchers.

    Healthcare Education Research Resources

    1. Health Education Research Journal (Oxford Academic)

      • A peer-reviewed journal that publishes research on health education and promotion, bridging the gap between research and practice in the field.

    2. Health Professions and Science Education Research Journals

      • A resource providing information on various journals focused on health professions and science education research, offering insights into current trends and studies.

    3. Stanford Health Education Library

      • An open-access library offering a wide range of health education resources, including videos and materials on topics like maternal and child health, COVID-19, and general health education.

    4. National Institutes of Health (NIH) Educational Resources

      • Provides a variety of educational resources related to clinical research, including videos, infographics, and information on participating in clinical studies.

    5. Health Education Research and Innovation Foundation (HERIF)

      • Supports high-impact programs aimed at transforming person-centered care through education, research, and innovation, providing resources and partnerships for healthcare professionals.
